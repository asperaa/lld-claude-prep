Step 1 · ~5 minutes
Requirements & Clarifications
Parking Lot is one of the most common LLD questions — but deceptively simple. The wrong move is to start drawing classes immediately. The right move is to interrogate the prompt. Each question you ask is a design decision you're making before writing any code. That's what senior engineers do.
The prompt you'll receive
"Design a parking lot system. Vehicles should be able to park and leave, and the system should track which spots are occupied."

This sentence leaves almost everything ambiguous. Does the lot have multiple floors? What vehicle types are supported? Is there a fee? Is there one entrance or many? Each answer changes a class you must write — or eliminates one you shouldn't.

Clarifying Questions — with deep reasoning

You
"What vehicle types should the system support — just cars, or also motorcycles, trucks, buses?"
Why this is your very first question: The answer determines whether ParkingSpot is a single class or a class hierarchy. If only cars exist, a spot is just a spot. But if motorcycles exist, a motorcycle can fit into a car spot (wasted capacity) or you might want dedicated small spots. If trucks exist, they might span multiple spots. This single question defines your entire entity model. Ask it before drawing anything.
Interviewer
"Support motorcycles, cars, and trucks. Each needs a different spot size: small, medium, large."
You
"Is this a multi-floor parking lot, or single level?"
Why this changes your structure: A single level means ParkingLot contains a flat list of spots. A multi-floor lot means you need a ParkingFloor entity between ParkingLot and ParkingSpot. Features like "park on floor closest to entrance" or "display floor-wise availability" only make sense with floors. If you skip this question and design for single-floor, you'll rebuild half the structure when the interviewer says "now add floor support."
Interviewer
"Multi-floor. Each floor has rows of spots of different sizes."
You
"Does the system charge a parking fee? If so, how — by time, flat rate, or vehicle type?"
Why this determines 2 whole classes: If no fee, your exit_vehicle() method is trivial — just mark the spot free. If fee exists, you need a Ticket (records entry time) and a FeeStrategy (calculates the fee at exit). These are not cosmetic additions — Ticket captures the moment of entry and without it, you cannot calculate duration. Without FeeStrategy, your fee calculation logic is hardcoded and changing the pricing model requires editing the core class. Ask this early.
Interviewer
"Yes, hourly fee. Different rates for different vehicle types."
You
"Are there multiple entrances and exits, or just one?"
Why this affects concurrency design: Multiple gates mean multiple threads could be calling park_vehicle() simultaneously. Two cars might be assigned the same spot if the assignment logic isn't thread-safe. With a single entrance, concurrency is simpler. This question tells you whether to add threading.Lock around spot assignment — a detail that will come up in follow-up questions.
Interviewer
"Multiple entrances and exits. Assume concurrent access."
You
"Should the system support reserved or handicapped spots — spots pre-assigned to specific vehicles or categories?"
Why this shapes your Strategy pattern: Reserved spots mean your "find a spot" logic cannot simply pick the first available medium spot — it must also check if the spot is reserved and if the vehicle is authorized. If you bake this logic into a single method now, it becomes an unmaintainable tangle of if/else. Knowing this early motivates you to use a ParkingStrategy interface with swappable implementations from the start.
Interviewer
"Yes — include handicapped spots and electric vehicle spots. Keep it extensible."
You
"What does a vehicle need to provide to enter — just its license plate, or a separate registration?"
Why the answer changes your Vehicle class: If a license plate is enough, Vehicle is minimal: just license_plate and vehicle_type. If the system pre-registers vehicles (like a monthly pass), you need an owner reference, subscription status, and pre-paid balance. This determines whether Vehicle is a value object (pure data) or a full domain entity with identity and behavior.
Interviewer
"Just license plate at entry. No pre-registration for now."

Scope Decision

In scope

  • Multi-floor parking lot with small / medium / large spots
  • Three vehicle types: motorcycle, car, truck
  • Park vehicle — find the best available spot and issue a ticket
  • Exit vehicle — compute fee based on duration and vehicle type
  • Spot availability display per floor
  • Special spots: handicapped, EV charging
  • Thread-safe assignment with multiple concurrent entrances
  • Strategy-based spot assignment (nearest, first available)

Out of scope

  • User accounts or vehicle pre-registration
  • Physical barrier/gate hardware integration
  • Payment processing (just calculate fee, not charge it)
  • Parking reservation / booking in advance
Step 2 · ~5 minutes
Core Entities & Responsibilities
Before writing any code, list every entity and what it owns. The key discipline: every piece of data belongs to exactly one entity. If data straddles two entities, you'll have duplication and sync bugs. If data belongs to none, it will end up as a random variable in a method body — unfindable later.
EntityWhat it ownsWhy it's a class, not just a field
ParkingLot List of floors, lot name/address, global available spot counts The orchestrator. All external operations — park, exit, get availability — go through here. It is the Facade that hides multi-floor complexity from the caller.
ParkingFloor Floor number, list of spots, per-floor availability counts Floors are independent units. You can query "floor 2 availability" or "first floor with a free large spot" without knowing about every spot in the building. Without ParkingFloor, ParkingLot holds a flat list of all spots — losing floor-level grouping forever.
ParkingSpot Spot ID, size, type (standard/handicapped/EV), status (free/occupied), current vehicle A spot enforces its own rules: it knows what size it accepts, whether it's available, and which vehicle is parked. These rules belong on the spot, not scattered in the lot's logic.
Vehicle License plate, vehicle type (motorcycle/car/truck) A value object with identity. Two vehicles are the same if their license plates match. The vehicle type drives spot assignment — a truck needs a large spot. This linkage belongs here, not in the spot or lot.
Ticket Ticket ID, vehicle, spot assigned, entry time, exit time, fee paid CriticalThis is the temporal record. Without Ticket, you cannot compute parking duration. At exit, you need entry time — and that time must be frozen at the moment of parking. A Ticket is the immutable receipt of "this vehicle entered at this time and was assigned this spot."
ParkingStrategy
(interface)		 Algorithm to find the best available spot Different lots have different policies: nearest to entrance, first available, keep premium spots for premium members. An interface lets you swap strategies without touching ParkingLot. Without this, all selection logic is hardcoded — changing the algorithm requires modifying the core class.
FeeCalculator
(interface)		 Algorithm to compute fee from duration and vehicle type Pricing changes constantly: weekends, peak hours, vehicle type multipliers. If fee logic is inside ParkingLot, every pricing change requires a regression test of the entire system. Extracted as a strategy, you test and swap it independently.
EnumTypes
(VehicleType, SpotSize, SpotType, SpotStatus)		 Valid values for categorical attributes Often skippedThese are not "nice to have." They make invalid states unrepresentable. You cannot accidentally write spot.size = "larg" (typo) if SpotSize is an Enum. The bug becomes a Python error at write time, not a runtime mismatch at midnight.
Why Ticket is not just a field on ParkingSpot

Beginners often think: "I'll just store entry_time directly on ParkingSpot. When the vehicle exits, I read it." This seems simpler — but it fails the moment you need history. Once the vehicle leaves and entry_time is cleared, the transaction is gone. Revenue reporting, dispute resolution ("I was only here 20 minutes, not 2 hours"), and audit logs are all impossible. A Ticket is a persistent, immutable record of a parking transaction. It survives the vehicle leaving. This is the same reason banks create transaction records, not just update an account balance.

Why SpotSize and VehicleType are separate Enums

Beginners often say "a motorcycle needs a small spot, so why not just use vehicle_type for the spot size?" Because the relationship between vehicle size and spot size is a policy, not a fundamental truth. A policy that "motorcycles can also park in medium spots if no small spot is available" is completely valid — and it would live in the ParkingStrategy. If you conflate vehicle type with spot size, this policy becomes impossible to express cleanly. Keep the two Enums separate: VehicleType describes what the vehicle is; SpotSize describes what the spot accepts.

Step 3 · ~15 minutes
Class Design — Every Line Justified
Design top-down: orchestrator first, then the most complex entities, then supporting types. For every field, ask: "what breaks if this doesn't exist?" For every method: "who else would own this if it weren't here?" If the answer is "nobody," it belongs here.

Enums — the Foundation

Write these before any class. Every categorical attribute in your domain should be an Enum. Enums are cheap to define and eliminate entire categories of bugs.

enums.py — VehicleType, SpotSize, SpotType, SpotStatus
from enum import Enum, auto class VehicleType(Enum): MOTORCYCLE = auto() CAR = auto() TRUCK = auto()
Why Enum instead of string or int constants? String constants like "motorcycle" are fragile: a typo ("motorcycl") passes silently — no error until runtime when a comparison fails. Integer constants (0, 1, 2) are even worse — they're meaningless without documentation. VehicleType.MOTORCYCLE is self-documenting and a typo is caught the moment you write it — Python raises AttributeError: MOTORCYCL immediately.
Without Enum: vehicle.type = "motorcyle" silently passes. Later, if vehicle.type == "motorcycle" never matches. The vehicle gets assigned to a car spot. No error, just wrong behavior.
class SpotSize(Enum): SMALL = auto() # motorcycles MEDIUM = auto() # cars LARGE = auto() # trucks
Why a separate SpotSize, not just use VehicleType? A spot's size is a physical fact about the spot. A vehicle's type is a fact about the vehicle. The relationship (which vehicles fit in which spots) is a policy. Conflating them means you can never express "a car can park in a large spot if no medium spots are free." Keep physical facts and policies separate.
class SpotType(Enum): STANDARD = auto() HANDICAPPED = auto() EV_CHARGING = auto()
Why SpotType is separate from SpotSize? A spot has two orthogonal attributes: how big it is (size) and what special features it has (type). A handicapped spot can be small (motorcycle) or medium (car). An EV charger can be on a large spot. These are independent dimensions. Collapsing them into one Enum forces ugly combinations like HANDICAPPED_MEDIUM — and adding EV charging to all sizes would require N new enum values. Two orthogonal axes need two Enums.
class SpotStatus(Enum): FREE = auto() OCCUPIED = auto() RESERVED = auto() BLOCKED = auto() # maintenance
Why RESERVED and BLOCKED beyond FREE/OCCUPIED? A real parking lot has more than two states. RESERVED means "this spot is assigned to a monthly pass holder" — it should never be given to a drive-in customer. BLOCKED means "under maintenance" — not a bug, not occupied, just temporarily unavailable. Without these, your assignment logic must invent workarounds. Capturing all real-world states in the Enum prevents hacks like spot.vehicle = dummy_vehicle to fake an "occupied" maintenance spot.

ParkingSpot — the Core Domain Object

parking_spot.py
class ParkingSpot: spot_id: str size: SpotSize spot_type: SpotType status: SpotStatus current_vehicle: Vehicle | None floor_number: int
Why does ParkingSpot know its floor_number? When you print a ticket or display "your car is at spot B-12 on floor 3," the spot must know its own location. Alternatively, you could look up the floor from the lot registry every time — but that couples display logic to a registry lookup, creating unnecessary dependencies. Storing floor_number directly on the spot makes it self-describing: the spot can identify itself fully without external lookups.
def is_available(self) -> bool: return (self.status == SpotStatus.FREE)
Why a method instead of checking status directly? Today, "available" means status == FREE. Tomorrow, it might mean status == FREE and not self.is_under_scheduled_maintenance(). If every caller checks spot.status == SpotStatus.FREE directly, you must update every one of them when the rule changes. With is_available(), you update one place. This is encapsulation — hide the implementation of "available" behind a method boundary.
def assign_vehicle(self, vehicle: Vehicle) -> None: if not self.is_available(): raise SpotUnavailableError( self.spot_id) self.current_vehicle = vehicle self.status = SpotStatus.OCCUPIED
Why does ParkingSpot own the assignment, not ParkingLot? The spot knows whether it's available. The rule "you can only assign a vehicle to an available spot" is a rule about the spot's own state — so it belongs on the spot. If ParkingLot sets spot.status = OCCUPIED directly from the outside, nothing stops it from skipping the availability check. Forcing assignment through spot.assign_vehicle() means the check is always enforced. This is the "Tell, Don't Ask" principle.
If ParkingLot set status directly: A race condition between two concurrent entries could assign the same spot twice. assign_vehicle() can be made atomic with a lock; direct status assignment cannot be so easily protected.
def release_vehicle(self ) -> Vehicle: vehicle = self.current_vehicle self.current_vehicle = None self.status = SpotStatus.FREE return vehicle
Why return the vehicle from release_vehicle()? The caller (exit flow in ParkingLot) needs to know which vehicle just left to look up its ticket, compute the fee, and record the transaction. If release_vehicle() returned nothing, the caller would need to read spot.current_vehicle before calling release — a fragile two-step that is easy to mess up or reverse in order. Returning the vehicle atomically in one method call is safer and cleaner.
def can_fit(self, vehicle: Vehicle) -> bool: required = SIZE_MAP[ vehicle.vehicle_type] return self.size == required
Why is can_fit() on ParkingSpot rather than Vehicle? The question "does this vehicle fit this spot?" involves both the spot's size and the vehicle's type. But the answer is from the spot's perspective — the spot is the one accepting or rejecting. Putting can_fit(vehicle) on the spot is natural: "can you (spot) fit this vehicle?" The alternative — vehicle.can_fit_in(spot) — creates a dependency from Vehicle to ParkingSpot, coupling two classes that otherwise have no connection. Avoid coupling in the direction of less control.

Ticket — the Transaction Record

ticket.py
class Ticket: ticket_id: str # UUID vehicle: Vehicle spot: ParkingSpot entry_time: datetime exit_time: datetime | None fee: float | None
Why store both the Vehicle and the Spot on Ticket? At exit, you need three things: (1) who was parked (vehicle, for the receipt), (2) where they were parked (spot, to release it), and (3) when they entered (entry_time, for fee calculation). The Ticket is the single place that knows all three. Without it, you'd need to reverse-engineer this from the spot's current vehicle — but once the vehicle is released, that information is gone.
Without Ticket: A truck exits. You release spot B-7. Now you need to compute the fee — but you don't know when it entered, because the only place that stored entry time was a variable in the park_vehicle() method that's now out of scope.
entry_time: datetime # Set at creation, never changed exit_time: datetime | None # None until vehicle exits
Why entry_time is immutable after creation? entry_time is a fact about when a transaction began. It should never change. If it's mutable, a bug (or a malicious actor) could backdate entries to reduce fees. Making it set-only at construction and never modified afterward is both safer and semantically correct. exit_time starts as None — not zero, not a placeholder — because None has explicit meaning: "this vehicle hasn't exited yet." A fee of None means "not yet calculated," distinct from a fee of 0.0.
def duration_hours(self ) -> float: end = self.exit_time or \ datetime.now() delta = end - self.entry_time return delta.total_seconds() \ / 3600
Why does duration_hours() live on Ticket? Duration is a derived property of the ticket's own data: exit_time - entry_time. No external class needs to know how to compute it. If FeeCalculator computed the duration itself, it would need access to both times — coupling the calculator to Ticket's internals. Instead, Ticket exposes a clean duration_hours() method. FeeCalculator just calls it. Encapsulation at the boundary.

ParkingLot — the Orchestrator

This is the Facade. External code talks only to ParkingLot. It owns registries, delegates to the right entities, and coordinates multi-step operations. It does not implement business rules — it delegates to spots, tickets, and strategies.

parking_lot.py — ParkingLot orchestrator
class ParkingLot: name: str floors: list[ParkingFloor] active_tickets: dict[ str, Ticket] # plate → ticket strategy: ParkingStrategy fee_calculator: FeeCalculator _lock: threading.Lock
Why dict[str, Ticket] keyed by license_plate? At exit, the vehicle scans its ticket or plate. The most natural lookup is "find the active ticket for this plate." A dict with plate as key gives O(1) lookup. An alternative — ticket ID — requires giving the driver a physical ticket to scan, which is also valid. License plate is simpler for LLD scope and avoids handling lost-ticket scenarios. Choose the key that matches your most common query pattern.
If you stored tickets in a list: Every exit scans all active tickets to find the matching plate. With 500 active vehicles, every exit is a 500-item scan. For a parking lot that processes thousands of exits per day, this is a performance problem with a trivially easy fix — use a dict.
strategy: ParkingStrategy fee_calculator: FeeCalculator
Why inject strategy and calculator instead of hardcoding? Different parking lots have different rules. A hospital lot might prioritize handicapped spots first, then nearest. An airport might charge flat rate regardless of vehicle type. A mall might charge by the hour, differently for cars vs trucks. These are interchangeable behaviors — the Strategy pattern. Injecting them means you can configure a ParkingLot differently for each use case without subclassing it. This is also Dependency Inversion: ParkingLot depends on the interface, not the implementation.
_lock: threading.Lock # protects spot assignment
Why a lock — and why only one? Two cars arriving simultaneously at different entrances both call park_vehicle(). Both find spot C-5 available (it's free at the moment both check). Both get assigned C-5. Now two cars are fighting over one spot — a race condition. The lock ensures only one thread runs the find-spot-and-assign block at a time. One lock is usually sufficient because spot assignment is fast (microseconds). If the lot is enormous, you could have per-floor locks — but that's a trade-off discussion, not the starting design.
def park_vehicle(self, vehicle: Vehicle ) -> Ticket: with self._lock: spot = self.strategy .find_spot( self.floors, vehicle) if spot is None: raise LotFullError() spot.assign_vehicle(vehicle) ticket = Ticket( vehicle=vehicle, spot=spot, entry_time=datetime.now()) self.active_tickets[ vehicle.license_plate ] = ticket return ticket
Why is Ticket creation outside the lock? The lock must protect the find-and-assign operation — that's the race condition window. Creating a Ticket object is pure in-memory work with no shared state — it's safe to do outside the lock. Keeping the lock scope as small as possible minimizes contention: other threads can proceed to look up floors, create objects, etc. while one thread holds the lock for just the critical 3 lines.
Principle: Lock for the minimum time needed to protect shared state. Locking more than necessary turns your concurrent system into an effectively single-threaded one.
def exit_vehicle(self, license_plate: str ) -> float: ticket = self.active_tickets .pop(license_plate, None) if ticket is None: raise TicketNotFoundError() ticket.exit_time = datetime.now() ticket.fee = self.fee_calculator .calculate(ticket) with self._lock: ticket.spot.release_vehicle() return ticket.fee
Why pop() instead of del — and why pop before the lock? .pop(plate, None) atomically removes and returns the ticket, or returns None if not found. This prevents a double-exit scenario: if two exit gates scan the same plate simultaneously, the first .pop() succeeds and the second gets None. del would require a separate .get() followed by del — two steps that are not atomic. Popping before the lock keeps the lock scope minimal: fee calculation and time-stamping happen outside the lock.

ParkingStrategy — the Swappable Algorithm

strategies.py — ParkingStrategy ABC and implementations
from abc import ABC, abstractmethod class ParkingStrategy(ABC): @abstractmethod def find_spot(self, floors: list[ParkingFloor], vehicle: Vehicle ) -> ParkingSpot | None: ...
Why ABC (Abstract Base Class) and not a plain class? ABC with @abstractmethod makes it impossible to instantiate ParkingStrategy directly — you must implement find_spot() in a subclass. This catches implementation mistakes at class definition time, not at runtime when find_spot() is first called. Without @abstractmethod, you could accidentally pass a plain ParkingStrategy() to ParkingLot. It would appear to work until the first call to find_spot() — which would return None from a no-op implementation, silently failing to find any spot.
class NearestSpotStrategy( ParkingStrategy): def find_spot(self, floors, vehicle): required = SIZE_MAP[ vehicle.vehicle_type] for floor in floors: for spot in floor.spots: if (spot.is_available() and spot.can_fit( vehicle)): return spot return None
Why return None instead of raising an exception? "No available spot" is a normal, expected business situation — not a programming error. Exceptions are for unexpected failures. None is a valid return signal meaning "I searched and found nothing." The caller (ParkingLot.park_vehicle()) then raises a user-visible LotFullError — which is the appropriate response to a full lot. Strategies don't know what to do when full; that's the orchestrator's responsibility.
class HandicappedFirstStrategy( ParkingStrategy): def find_spot(self, floors, vehicle): # First: look for handicapped spot for floor in floors: for spot in floor.spots: if (spot.is_available() and spot.can_fit(vehicle) and spot.spot_type == SpotType.HANDICAPPED): return spot # Fallback: regular spot return NearestSpotStrategy( ).find_spot(floors, vehicle)
Why compose with NearestSpotStrategy instead of duplicating code? The fallback logic — "if no handicapped spot, find the nearest regular one" — is identical to NearestSpotStrategy. Duplicating that logic here violates DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself). If you later change how "nearest" is defined, you'd need to update both places. Composing by instantiating and calling NearestSpotStrategy keeps the duplication in exactly zero places. This is also an example of the Strategy pattern composing with itself — powerful and clean.

FeeCalculator — Pricing as a Strategy

fee_calculator.py
class FeeCalculator(ABC): @abstractmethod def calculate(self, ticket: Ticket) -> float: ... class HourlyFeeCalculator( FeeCalculator): RATES = { VehicleType.MOTORCYCLE: 20, VehicleType.CAR: 50, VehicleType.TRUCK: 100, } def calculate(self, ticket: Ticket) -> float: hours = math.ceil( ticket.duration_hours()) rate = self.RATES[ ticket.vehicle.vehicle_type] return hours * rate
Why math.ceil() and not round()? Parking lots charge for any part of an hour started. If you park for 1 hour 5 minutes, you pay for 2 hours. math.ceil(1.083) = 2. round(1.083) = 1. The difference is ₹50 per car — at 500 cars/day, that's ₹25,000/day of lost revenue if you use the wrong rounding. Rounding mode is a business rule, not a math preference. Know why you chose it.
With round(): A 1-hour 59-minute stay rounds to 2 hours (correct). A 2-hour 1-minute stay also rounds to 2 hours — the lot eats that extra minute free. Over millions of transactions, this accumulates into significant revenue loss.
Step 4 · ~10 minutes
Key Flows — Step by Step
Walk through the two most important operations in the system: parking a vehicle and exiting. For each step, understand not just what happens but which entity is responsible and why it's not a different entity.

Flow 1 — park_vehicle(vehicle)

Complete park_vehicle() sequence
Step 1: Acquire the lock with self._lock:
Why is the lock the very first thing? The entire find-and-assign operation must be atomic. If Thread A finds spot C-5 is free, then Thread B also finds C-5 is free before A has assigned it — both threads try to park in the same spot. The lock ensures that between "find" and "assign," no other thread can touch the shared spot state. First instruction, no exceptions.
Step 2: Validate no double-park if plate in self.active_tickets: raise AlreadyParkedError()
Why check before finding a spot? A vehicle that's already parked shouldn't get a second spot. This check prevents a vehicle from parking twice, which would mean one vehicle holds two spots and generates two tickets — both revenue loss and physical impossibility. Check this early so you fail fast with a clear error, before running the (potentially expensive) spot-finding algorithm.
Step 3: Delegate to strategy spot = self.strategy.find_spot( self.floors, vehicle) if spot is None: raise LotFullError()
Why does ParkingLot not search for spots itself? ParkingLot is the orchestrator, not the algorithm. "How to find the best available spot" is a separate responsibility. Today you want nearest-first. Tomorrow a premium customer wants their reserved spot on floor 1. The strategy varies; the orchestration does not. ParkingLot delegates the how to a strategy and just acts on the result.
Step 4: Spot assigns itself spot.assign_vehicle(vehicle) # releases lock after this line
Why does the spot assign itself instead of ParkingLot setting its status? The spot enforces its own invariants. assign_vehicle() checks availability, sets the vehicle, and updates the status atomically. If ParkingLot set spot.status = OCCUPIED directly, it could bypass the availability check. The spot's method is the only way in — and it's the only place this rule needs to live. Tell, Don't Ask.
Step 5: Create Ticket (outside lock) ticket = Ticket( vehicle=vehicle, spot=spot, entry_time=datetime.now()) self.active_tickets[ vehicle.license_plate] = ticket return ticket
Why is Ticket creation after the lock is released? The lock protects spot state — shared mutable data. Ticket creation is pure object construction with no shared state. Holding the lock while creating objects, registering the ticket, and logging would block other threads unnecessarily. Minimise the lock scope: lock for the critical section, release, then do everything else.

Flow 2 — exit_vehicle(license_plate)

Complete exit_vehicle() sequence
Step 1: Remove ticket atomically ticket = self.active_tickets.pop( license_plate, None) if ticket is None: raise TicketNotFoundError()
Why pop() first, before acquiring the lock? dict.pop() in Python is thread-safe for a single operation (GIL-protected). Popping the ticket first eliminates the race condition where two exit threads process the same vehicle simultaneously — the second pop() gets None and raises an error immediately. This is a cheap guard that prevents double-processing before we even need the more expensive lock.
Step 2: Record exit time first ticket.exit_time = datetime.now()
Why record exit time before calculating fee? Fee calculation uses ticket.duration_hours(), which computes exit_time - entry_time. If you calculate the fee before setting exit_time, duration_hours() falls back to datetime.now() — but by the time the fee is calculated, a few more milliseconds have passed. The fee is slightly wrong. Set the exit timestamp first, then calculate from it. Sequence matters.
Step 3: Calculate fee ticket.fee = self.fee_calculator .calculate(ticket)
Why delegate to fee_calculator rather than computing inline? Fee calculation is a business rule that changes — often, and independently of parking mechanics. Delegating to fee_calculator means you can test pricing logic in complete isolation, swap it without touching ParkingLot, and configure different lots with different pricing. If you computed the fee inline in exit_vehicle(), the pricing logic would be forever coupled to this method.
Step 4: Release spot (under lock) with self._lock: ticket.spot.release_vehicle() return ticket.fee
Why lock just the release, not the whole exit flow? Only the moment of marking a spot free needs synchronization — another thread's park_vehicle() must not see a spot as "free" before it actually is. Fee calculation and ticket updates don't modify shared spot state, so they don't need the lock. The lock scope is as small as possible: just release_vehicle() — one method call on one object. Anything more is unnecessary contention.
The full state of a Ticket across its lifecycle
Created→ exit_time set→ fee calculated→ spot released→ Closed

Each step happens in this exact order. Reversing step 3 and 4 (calculating fee after releasing the spot) is harmless here — but good practice to release resources as late as possible after you've finished reading them. The spot reference in the ticket is still valid even after release, since Python doesn't garbage-collect the object while a reference exists.

Step 5 · Deep Dive
Design Patterns — with Concrete Whys
Every pattern in this design was chosen to solve a specific problem. Don't memorize patterns — understand the problem each one solves. In an interview, naming the pattern impresses; explaining why it was needed here gets you the offer.
1. Singleton Creational

Where it's used: ParkingLot itself — there is exactly one physical parking lot instance in the system.

The problem it solves: If someone creates two ParkingLot instances, they have two separate registries of active tickets and two separate spot states. A vehicle parked through instance 1 is invisible to instance 2. The lot appears to be full from one instance and empty from the other. This is a split-brain problem.

The implementation:

class ParkingLot: _instance = None _lock = threading.Lock() @classmethod def get_instance(cls) -> 'ParkingLot': if cls._instance is None: with cls._lock: # double-checked locking if cls._instance is None: cls._instance = ParkingLot() return cls._instance

Why double-checked locking: The outer if avoids acquiring the lock on every call (which would be expensive). The inner if (inside the lock) handles the rare race condition where two threads both pass the outer check simultaneously. Both checks together give you the safety of locking without the constant cost of it.

When to skip it: In modern Python, if you're using dependency injection frameworks or testing, Singleton can make unit testing painful (tests share state). A common alternative is to just use a module-level instance — Python modules are singletons by nature.

2. Strategy Behavioural

Where it's used: ParkingStrategy (spot selection) and FeeCalculator (pricing).

The problem it solves: A parking lot in a hospital needs handicapped spots first. A mall lot needs nearest-to-entrance. An airport lot charges flat rate. A city lot charges hourly with peak pricing. These are different algorithms for the same operation. Without Strategy, you write one giant method with if lot_type == "hospital": ... elif lot_type == "mall": ... — a mess that grows every time a new lot type is added.

❌ Without Strategy
def find_spot(self, vehicle):
    if self.type == "hospital":
        # check handicapped first
        ...
    elif self.type == "mall":
        # nearest entrance
        ...
    elif self.type == "vip":
        # reserved spots first
        ...
Every new lot type requires editing this method. Adding "airport strategy" risks breaking hospital logic. Testing hospital logic requires constructing the entire ParkingLot with type="hospital".
✓ With Strategy
lot = ParkingLot(
  strategy=HandicappedFirst(),
  fee_calc=HourlyCalculator())

# Or for airport:
lot = ParkingLot(
  strategy=NearestSpot(),
  fee_calc=FlatRateCalculator())
Each strategy is tested in isolation. Adding a new strategy is a new class — zero changes to ParkingLot. Open/Closed Principle satisfied completely.
3. Factory Method Creational

Where it's used: VehicleFactory.create(plate, type_str) — creating the right Vehicle subclass from external input.

The problem it solves: At the entrance gate, a human types "CAR" into a terminal. Your code receives a string. You need a Car object. If you write if type == "CAR": return Car(plate) everywhere, that if/else is duplicated every place a vehicle is created. The Factory centralizes this mapping.

class VehicleFactory: @staticmethod def create(plate: str, type_str: str) -> Vehicle: mapping = { "MOTORCYCLE": Motorcycle, "CAR": Car, "TRUCK": Truck, } cls = mapping.get( type_str.upper()) if cls is None: raise ValueError( f"Unknown: {type_str}") return cls(plate)

Why the dict-based mapping instead of if/else: Adding a new vehicle type (e.g., "BUS") is one line: add "BUS": Bus to the dict. With if/else, you must find the right elif and insert correctly. The dict is data; if/else is code. Data is easier to extend and test.

4. Observer Behavioural

Where it's used: Notifying display boards and availability counters when a spot's status changes.

The problem it solves: When a car parks, the entrance display board should update ("Floor 2: 14 spots available"). When it leaves, it should update again. If ParkingSpot.assign_vehicle() directly calls the display board, the spot is coupled to a display system — they become impossible to test or change independently. Observer decouples them: the spot fires an event; whoever is listening handles it.

class SpotStatusObserver(ABC): @abstractmethod def on_spot_changed(self, spot: ParkingSpot, new_status: SpotStatus): ... class DisplayBoardObserver( SpotStatusObserver): def on_spot_changed(self, spot, new_status): # Update LED display for floor self.display.refresh( spot.floor_number) class AvailabilityCounterObserver( SpotStatusObserver): def on_spot_changed(self, spot, new_status): if new_status == SpotStatus.FREE: self.counts[ spot.floor_number] += 1 else: self.counts[ spot.floor_number] -= 1

The key benefit: Adding a new reaction to spot changes — say, sending an SMS alert when a floor hits 5% capacity — is a new SpotStatusObserver subclass. Nothing in ParkingSpot, ParkingFloor, or ParkingLot changes. This is the extension-without-modification guarantee of the Open/Closed Principle, delivered by Observer.

5. State Behavioural

Where it's used: ParkingSpot's status transitions (FREE → OCCUPIED → FREE, and variants).

The problem it solves: A spot's behavior changes depending on its state. In state FREE: assign_vehicle() works. In state OCCUPIED: it should raise. In state BLOCKED: even release_vehicle() should be blocked. Without the State pattern, you write if self.status == FREE: ... elif self.status == BLOCKED: ... inside every method. Every new status means editing every method.

The lightweight version (used here): A VALID_TRANSITIONS dict, similar to Swiggy's Order state machine. Simpler than full State objects but captures the same invariants:

VALID_TRANSITIONS = { SpotStatus.FREE: [SpotStatus.OCCUPIED, SpotStatus.RESERVED, SpotStatus.BLOCKED], SpotStatus.OCCUPIED: [SpotStatus.FREE], SpotStatus.RESERVED: [SpotStatus.OCCUPIED, SpotStatus.FREE], SpotStatus.BLOCKED: [SpotStatus.FREE], # only ops team can unblock } def _transition(self, new_status: SpotStatus): allowed = VALID_TRANSITIONS.get( self.status, []) if new_status not in allowed: raise InvalidSpotTransitionError( self.status, new_status) self.status = new_status

Why a dict, not if/else: BLOCKED → OCCUPIED is missing from the dict intentionally. A maintenance spot cannot be directly occupied — it must be unblocked first. This constraint is data, not code. You read the dict to understand the rules; you don't need to trace through branching logic.

6. Template Method Behavioural

Where it's used: A base FeeCalculator that handles duration extraction, while subclasses implement the per-vehicle rate lookup.

The problem it solves: Every fee calculator needs to: (1) get duration from ticket, (2) apply ceiling rounding, (3) look up the rate for the vehicle type, (4) multiply. Steps 1, 2, 4 are the same for all calculators. Step 3 is the variable part. Template Method puts the fixed skeleton in the base class and delegates the variable part (rate lookup) to subclasses.

class FeeCalculator(ABC): def calculate(self, ticket: Ticket) -> float: # Fixed skeleton — same for all hours = math.ceil( ticket.duration_hours()) rate = self.get_rate( # ← variable part ticket.vehicle.vehicle_type) return hours * rate @abstractmethod def get_rate(self, vtype: VehicleType) -> float: ... # subclasses define this class WeekdayCalculator(FeeCalculator): def get_rate(self, vtype): return {MOTORCYCLE: 20, CAR: 50, TRUCK: 100 }[vtype] class WeekendCalculator(FeeCalculator): def get_rate(self, vtype): return {MOTORCYCLE: 30, CAR: 80, TRUCK: 150 }[vtype] # weekend premium

Why this matters: If you later need a peak-hours calculator, you write PeakHoursCalculator(FeeCalculator) and implement get_rate(). The rounding logic, duration extraction — all inherited automatically. You write only the part that changes. This is what "code reuse through inheritance" actually means in practice.

Step 6 · Deep Dive
SOLID Principles — Applied to Parking Lot
SOLID principles are not abstract rules — they are consequences of designing for change. Every principle points at a specific category of change that your design must accommodate gracefully. Here's where each principle appears in this system, and why violating it would hurt.
S
Single Responsibility Principle
"A class should have one reason to change."

Applied: ParkingSpot manages spot state. FeeCalculator manages pricing. Ticket records transactions. ParkingStrategy selects spots. None of these does another's job.

Violation: If ParkingSpot also computed fees, it would change whenever the business changes pricing rules — even though the spot itself hasn't changed at all. Two unrelated reasons to change = SRP violation.
Result of following SRP: Pricing logic changes? Only FeeCalculator changes. Spot state management changes? Only ParkingSpot. Each class has exactly one owner, one team, one reason to be touched.
O
Open/Closed Principle
"Open for extension, closed for modification."

Applied: Adding a new vehicle type, spot selection strategy, or fee model does not require modifying ParkingLot. You add a new subclass of ParkingStrategy or FeeCalculator.

Violation: If park_vehicle() had if vehicle.type == "MOTORCYCLE": find_small_spot() elif vehicle.type == "TRUCK": find_large_spot() — adding a BUS type requires editing park_vehicle() and potentially introducing regressions for existing types.
Result: A new EVPriorityStrategy (park EVs near charging spots first) is one new class. Zero lines in ParkingLot change. The system is extended, never broken.
L
Liskov Substitution Principle
"Subclasses must be substitutable for their base class."

Applied: HandicappedFirstStrategy and NearestSpotStrategy both implement find_spot(floors, vehicle) → ParkingSpot | None. Either can be passed to ParkingLot with identical behavior from the caller's perspective.

Violation: If HandicappedFirstStrategy.find_spot() raised an exception for trucks (instead of returning None), the caller couldn't safely use it interchangeably. Substitution would break the system silently.
Test for LSP: Every concrete strategy must return a ParkingSpot or None. Never raise instead of returning None. Never add extra required parameters. The contract is fixed by the base class.
I
Interface Segregation Principle
"Clients shouldn't depend on methods they don't use."

Applied: ParkingStrategy has one method: find_spot(). FeeCalculator has one method: calculate(). SpotStatusObserver has one method: on_spot_changed(). No interface forces a class to implement methods it doesn't need.

Violation: If you combined ParkingStrategy and FeeCalculator into one interface ParkingPolicy, every strategy must implement a calculate() method even if it has nothing to do with fees — or implement a do-nothing stub. Both are bad.
Rule of thumb: If a class implementing your interface has to write raise NotImplementedError for some methods, your interface is too fat. Split it.
D
Dependency Inversion Principle
"Depend on abstractions, not concretions."

Applied: ParkingLot depends on ParkingStrategy (abstract) and FeeCalculator (abstract), not on NearestSpotStrategy or HourlyFeeCalculator (concrete). The concrete implementations are injected at construction time.

❌ Violation — depends on concrete
class ParkingLot:
    def __init__(self):
        # hardwired to a specific strategy
        self.strategy = NearestSpotStrategy()
        self.fee_calc = HourlyFeeCalculator()
Testing ParkingLot requires NearestSpotStrategy to work. Changing to HandicappedFirstStrategy requires editing ParkingLot's __init__. Two classes are welded together.
✓ DIP — depends on abstractions
class ParkingLot:
    def __init__(self,
        strategy: ParkingStrategy,
        fee_calc: FeeCalculator):
        self.strategy = strategy
        self.fee_calc = fee_calc
Test with a MockStrategy that always returns spot #1. Swap to HandicappedFirstStrategy with zero code changes to ParkingLot. The lot doesn't even know which implementation it's using.

The testability proof: With DIP, a unit test for park_vehicle() injects a MockStrategy that immediately returns a predetermined spot. No floors, no real spots needed. The test is 10 lines and runs in milliseconds. Without DIP, you must construct the entire lot with real floors and real spots just to test one method.

Step 7 · Senior-Level Discussion
Trade-offs — Show You Think Like an Engineer
Every design decision has a cost. An interviewer asking "why did you do X instead of Y?" is probing whether you know what you gave up. Here are the key trade-offs in this design, each explained with both sides.
One global lock vs. per-floor locks
The situation: Multiple cars entering simultaneously at different entrances. We need to prevent two cars from being assigned the same spot.
✓ One global lock (current design)Simpler code. No deadlock risk (only one lock in the system). For most parking lots, spot assignment is so fast (microseconds) that contention on one lock is negligible. Correct by default.
⚠ Per-floor locks (alternative)Higher throughput: two cars entering on different floors can be assigned simultaneously. But now you have multiple locks — risk of deadlock if any code accidentally acquires floor locks in different orders. More complex to implement and test. Justified only if profiling shows lock contention is actually a bottleneck.

Decision rule: Start with the simplest correct design. Profile before optimizing. A parking lot processes tens of cars per hour — per-floor locks are premature optimization unless you're designing for an enormous automated garage with sub-second throughput requirements.

Motorcycle fits in a medium spot — policy or not?
The situation: The lot is full of small spots, but medium spots are available. Should a motorcycle be allowed to use a medium spot?
✓ Strict sizing (current design)Simple can_fit(): motorcycle → small, car → medium, truck → large. No ambiguity. Easy to understand, test, and explain. Revenue is predictable.
⚠ Flexible sizing (alternative)A motorcycle in a medium spot is revenue — better than an empty spot. But now can_fit() needs a priority order: "prefer small, allow medium." This logic lives in the strategy, not the spot — which is actually cleaner. The strategy can implement "best fit" or "first fit" with size flexibility.

How to answer in interview: "I've kept can_fit() strict for clarity. Flexible sizing would be a configurable policy in the strategy — the strategy could include a SIZE_COMPATIBILITY map: MOTORCYCLE: [SMALL, MEDIUM]. The spot class doesn't change — only the strategy's logic does." This shows you've thought it through without over-engineering the initial design.

Keying active_tickets by license_plate vs. ticket_id
The situation: At exit, you look up the active ticket. Should you look up by the vehicle's license plate or by a physical ticket ID the driver carries?
✓ License plate (current)No physical ticket to lose. Works for automated ANPR (number plate recognition) gates. Simpler UX — drive in, drive out, plate is scanned automatically both ways.
⚠ Ticket ID (alternative)More common in real lots. Supports valet parking (different person parks and exits). Supports one vehicle making multiple trips in a day. Requires handling lost-ticket scenario. Requires a separate active_tickets: dict[ticket_id, Ticket] lookup.

Best answer: "I've chosen license plate for simplicity. A production system would likely support both — ANPR for automated gates and ticket ID as fallback. Both lookups are O(1) with dicts; we'd just maintain two dicts or one with two index structures."

In-memory state vs. database persistence
The situation: This design keeps all state in-memory (Python dicts). What happens if the server crashes mid-parking-session?
✓ In-memory (LLD scope)Simple, fast, sufficient for interview scope. Demonstrates design concepts without infrastructure complexity. O(1) lookups, no DB calls, no connection pooling. Appropriate for the question asked.
⚠ Production realityA server crash loses all active ticket data. A vehicle is parked but no exit record exists — the spot stays "occupied" forever. Production systems need persistent storage (DB), with Ticket as a DB record and spot status replicated from tickets. But this is HLD territory — flag it and move on in an LLD interview.

How to handle this question: "This is an in-memory design appropriate for LLD. In production, I'd persist Tickets to a database (the Ticket entity maps cleanly to a DB row). Spot availability could be derived from active tickets, or cached in Redis with the DB as source of truth for recovery."

Step 8 · Interview Simulation
Interview Q&A — Likely Follow-ups
These are the questions most likely to follow after you present your design. Each answer is grounded in the design decisions made earlier — notice how every answer traces back to a deliberate choice.
Why did you use an abstract class for ParkingStrategy instead of a plain function?

A plain function would work for a single strategy — but a strategy often needs state. Consider a PreferredFloorStrategy that always tries floor 1 first — it needs to know which floor is "preferred," configurable per lot. A class can hold that configuration: PreferredFloorStrategy(preferred_floor=1). A plain function has no home for configuration — it either hardcodes it or takes extra parameters on every call (leaking configuration concerns into the caller).

Additionally, an ABC enforces the contract at the class level. If someone writes a new strategy and forgets to implement find_spot(), Python raises TypeError: Can't instantiate abstract class the moment they try to use it — not at runtime during a vehicle lookup. The ABC is a compile-time (or import-time) contract, not a documentation hope.

How would you handle a truck that needs two consecutive spots?

This is an extension to the ParkingStrategy — specifically a MultiSpotStrategy. The key changes are:

On ParkingSpot: Add an optional adjacent_spot_id: str | None field. When a floor is laid out, adjacent spots are linked. A strategy can traverse this link to find two consecutive free spots.

On Ticket: Change spot: ParkingSpot to spots: list[ParkingSpot]. A truck occupying two spots has a ticket with two spot references. Exit releases both.

On FeeCalculator: The rate for TRUCK already accounts for the size. But if you want to charge per spot used, calculate(ticket) can multiply by len(ticket.spots).

Critical observation: ParkingLot.park_vehicle() and ParkingSpot.assign_vehicle() don't change their signatures — the multi-spot logic is entirely inside the new strategy. This is the payoff of the Strategy pattern: complex behaviors are additive, not disruptive.

class MultiSpotStrategy(ParkingStrategy): def find_spot(self, floors, vehicle): if vehicle.vehicle_type != VehicleType.TRUCK: return NearestSpotStrategy().find_spot(floors, vehicle) for floor in floors: for i, spot in enumerate(floor.spots[:-1]): next_spot = floor.spots[i + 1] if (spot.is_available() and next_spot.is_available() and spot.size == SpotSize.LARGE and next_spot.size == SpotSize.LARGE): return (spot, next_spot) # return tuple return None
How would you add a monthly pass — vehicles that pay flat monthly fee and get priority parking?

This touches three parts of the design — each change is surgical and doesn't break existing behavior:

On Vehicle: Add is_monthly_pass: bool and pass_expiry: datetime | None. A helper method has_valid_pass() -> bool checks both the flag and expiry date.

New Strategy: MonthlyPassPriorityStrategy — checks vehicle.has_valid_pass(). If true, it looks for RESERVED spots first (pre-designated for pass holders), then falls back to regular nearest spots. If false, it delegates directly to NearestSpotStrategy.

On FeeCalculator: PassAwareFeeCalculator — checks ticket.vehicle.has_valid_pass(). If true, returns 0.0 (pass covers this visit) or a discounted rate. If false, delegates to HourlyFeeCalculator.

Key insight for the interviewer: ParkingLot itself does not change. You configure it with the new strategy and calculator at construction. The lot is oblivious to the existence of monthly passes — the business logic lives entirely in the strategy and calculator. This is dependency inversion and the open/closed principle working together.

Why is can_fit() on ParkingSpot and not on ParkingLot?

This is the Tell, Don't Ask principle — the same reason can_cancel() lives on Order in Swiggy's design, not on SwiggySystem.

The alternative: ParkingLot (or ParkingStrategy) checks if spot.size == SIZE_MAP[vehicle.type] directly everywhere it needs to verify fit. This works. But now the rule "this size spot can fit this vehicle type" is scattered across every caller instead of owned by the spot.

The concrete risk: You add a new rule: "motorcycles can also park in medium spots if the lot is 90% full." Now you must find and update every location that checks spot-vehicle compatibility. With can_fit() on the spot, you update one method. Every caller — NearestSpotStrategy, HandicappedFirstStrategy, any future strategy — automatically gets the new rule for free.

The general principle: Rules about an entity's own attributes belong on that entity. A spot's size is its own attribute. The rule "does this vehicle fit given my size" is a rule about the spot's size. It belongs on ParkingSpot.

Walk me through the complete sequence when a car exits and there's a dispute about the fee.

This tests whether your design produces an auditable record. Here's the sequence:

1. Exit triggered: ParkingLot.exit_vehicle("KA05-HJ-7890") is called. The ticket is popped from active_tickets.

2. Timestamp recorded: ticket.exit_time = datetime.now(). This is the official exit time — immutable from this point.

3. Fee computed: fee_calculator.calculate(ticket) uses ticket.entry_time and ticket.exit_time to compute duration, applies ceiling rounding, looks up the vehicle type rate, multiplies. Returns ₹150.

4. Dispute scenario: The driver claims they parked for 1 hour but are being charged for 2. The Ticket object (and its DB record in production) has both entry_time and exit_time preserved. The gate operator can show: "entry 14:03, exit 15:05 — 62 minutes, rounded up to 2 hours = ₹100." The Ticket is the receipt, the evidence, and the audit trail.

5. Spot released: After fee is recorded on the ticket, ticket.spot.release_vehicle() marks the spot FREE. The spot is now available for the next vehicle. If the release fails for any reason, the fee record still exists on the Ticket — the transaction is preserved even if cleanup fails.

Key takeaway: The Ticket is never modified after creation except to add exit_time and fee at the end. It is an append-only record. This is what makes dispute resolution possible — the data was frozen at the right moments.

How would you make this design fully unit-testable?

Testability is a direct consequence of Dependency Inversion. Here's what a test for park_vehicle() looks like when DIP is applied:

def test_park_vehicle_assigns_spot_and_issues_ticket(): # Arrange: build a minimal lot with a known spot spot = ParkingSpot( spot_id="A-1", size=SpotSize.MEDIUM, spot_type=SpotType.STANDARD, floor_number=1) floor = ParkingFloor( floor_number=1, spots=[spot]) # Inject a mock strategy that always returns spot A-1 mock_strategy = MockStrategy( always_returns=spot) lot = ParkingLot( floors=[floor], strategy=mock_strategy, fee_calc=HourlyFeeCalculator()) car = Car(license_plate="KA01-AB-1234") # Act ticket = lot.park_vehicle(car) # Assert assert ticket.vehicle == car assert ticket.spot == spot assert ticket.exit_time is None # still parked assert spot.status == SpotStatus.OCCUPIED assert "KA01-AB-1234" in lot.active_tickets

This test: runs in milliseconds, requires zero physical spots or floors beyond what we explicitly set up, uses a MockStrategy to control what find_spot() returns, and tests exactly one behavior — that park_vehicle() assigns the spot and records the ticket correctly. This precision is only possible because every dependency is injected. Without DIP, you cannot instantiate ParkingLot without instantiating every strategy and calculator — and you lose control over what they return.

How would you display real-time availability on entrance boards?

This is the Observer pattern in action, and the answer traces directly back to a design decision already made.

The mechanism: ParkingSpot maintains a list of SpotStatusObserver instances. Every time assign_vehicle() or release_vehicle() is called, the spot notifies all observers via obs.on_spot_changed(self, new_status).

The DisplayBoardObserver: Maintains a dict[floor_number, int] of free spot counts. On FREE → OCCUPIED, decrements the count. On OCCUPIED → FREE, increments. Exposes get_availability(floor_n) → int for the display board to read.

The AvailabilityAlertObserver: When the count drops below 5%, sends an SMS alert to the lot manager. Adding this requires zero changes to ParkingSpot, ParkingFloor, or ParkingLot. You register it at startup: spot.add_observer(AvailabilityAlertObserver(threshold=0.05)).

Thread safety note: Observer callbacks fire inside assign_vehicle() and release_vehicle(), which are called under the lock. This means observer code also runs under the lock — keep observer implementations fast and non-blocking. If an observer needs to do heavy work (like sending an HTTP request to update an external board), it should enqueue the work for a background thread rather than doing it inline.
