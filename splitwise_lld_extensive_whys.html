Step 1 · ~5 minutes
Requirements & Clarifications
The interviewer gives you a vague sentence. Your first job is not to design — it is to interrogate. Every question you ask is a bug you're preventing before you write a single class. Notice how little the prompt below actually specifies.
The prompt you'll receive
"Design a system like Splitwise that allows users to split expenses in a group and settle debts."

That sentence leaves almost everything ambiguous. What split types must be supported — just equal, or also exact amounts and percentages? How should debts be settled — naively or optimally? Who creates groups? These aren't details — each answer changes a class you must write or eliminates one you shouldn't.

Clarifying Questions — with deep reasoning

You
"What split types do we need to support? I'm thinking equal at minimum, but real Splitwise also has exact amounts and percentages. Should I design for all three?"
Why this is the most important question to ask first: The answer determines whether your Split class is a simple float wrapper or an extensible hierarchy. "Just equal" means one class, hardcoded logic. "All three" means you need a Strategy pattern — an abstract base with three concrete implementations. If you assume equal-only and the interviewer meant all three, you'll rebuild half your design. If you assume all three and they wanted one, you've over-engineered. One question, asked now, saves twenty minutes later.
Interviewer
"Yes — support all three. Equal split, exact amounts, and percentage split."
You
"When someone adds an expense, does every group member participate, or can it be a subset of members?"
Why "subset" changes your data model: If all members always participate, the split logic is simple — take the full list. But if a subset can be chosen, add_expense() must accept a participants argument, and your split objects must record which user each belongs to — not just an amount. This is actually the correct design regardless (you need to know who owes what), but clarifying it forces you to think about it explicitly instead of discovering it mid-implementation.
Interviewer
"Either — an expense can involve all members or just a subset."
You
"For the settlement — should the system just show raw balances per pair, or should it compute an optimized plan that minimizes the number of transactions?"
Why "minimize transactions" is the hard problem: Showing raw balances is trivial — iterate the balance dict. Minimizing transactions requires a real algorithm. If Alice owes Bob ₹300 and Bob owes Carol ₹300, the optimal plan is Alice pays Carol directly — one transaction instead of two. At scale with 10 people and 30 expenses, the naive approach produces O(n²) transactions while the optimal produces at most n−1. This is the algorithmically interesting part of the problem — it tells you whether you need a greedy heap or just a for loop.
Interviewer
"Minimize transactions — that's the interesting part."
You
"For percentage splits, should I validate that percentages sum to exactly 100? For exact splits, that amounts sum to the total?"
Why validation belongs in the question, not in an afterthought: If you add validation mid-design, you have to decide retroactively where it lives — in the Split subclass? In the factory? In add_expense()? Each answer has different implications. Asking now means you design with validation in mind from the start, so it lands in the right place (the factory, as we'll see) rather than being scattered. It also signals to the interviewer that you think about data integrity, not just happy paths.
Interviewer
"Yes — reject invalid splits with a clear error. Percentages must sum to 100, exact amounts must sum to the total."
You
"Do we need to handle recording that a payment was actually made — i.e., marking a debt as settled — or just computing what's owed?"
Why recording payments is a separate concern from computing them: Computing what's owed is read-only — it derives from existing expenses. Recording a payment is a write operation that changes the balance state. Without it, the system can tell you "Bob owes Alice ₹500" but can never update that when Bob actually pays. The balance sheet becomes permanently out of sync with reality. This question determines whether record_payment() is in scope and whether your balance data structure must support bi-directional updates.
Interviewer
"Yes — include recording payments so the balance updates when someone settles up."
You
"Should I worry about persistent storage, a REST API, authentication, or currency conversion?"
Why calling out what's NOT in scope is as valuable as what is: In a 35-minute interview, spending 10 minutes on currency conversion or JWT auth means you never get to the settlement algorithm — the interesting part. Stating out-of-scope items explicitly does three things: (1) proves you thought about them and made a deliberate choice, not an accidental omission; (2) protects you from scope creep mid-design; (3) makes the design look intentionally bounded rather than incomplete. Always end requirements with explicit exclusions.
Interviewer
"No — in-memory is fine. Focus on the object model and algorithms."

Final Requirements

In scope

  • Register users, create groups, add members to groups.
  • Add an expense: who paid, how much, split type, and which participants.
  • Three split types: Equal (amount ÷ n), Exact (caller specifies each share), Percentage (caller specifies %).
  • Validate splits: percentages must sum to 100, exact amounts must sum to the total.
  • View current balance for any user in a group (who owes whom, how much).
  • Generate an optimized settlement plan that minimizes the number of transactions.
  • Record that a payment was made — balance updates accordingly.

Out of scope

  • Persistent storage or database.
  • REST API or HTTP layer.
  • Authentication and authorization.
  • Currency conversion.
  • Expense editing or deletion.
  • Notifications of any kind.
The three sub-problems hidden inside "split expenses"

Before designing any class, recognize that this prompt contains three distinct algorithmic problems: (1) How do you divide a payment? — the split strategies. (2) How do you track what everyone owes over time? — the balance data structure. (3) How do you produce the minimum transactions to settle all debts? — the settlement algorithm. These three problems map to three separate design decisions. Getting any one wrong produces a specific, concrete failure. Recognizing this structure before writing a single class is a senior-level signal.

Step 2 · ~5 minutes
Core Entities & Relationships
Read the requirements and circle every noun. That is your candidate list. Now apply a ruthless filter: does this noun have state that changes over time AND behavior that enforces rules on that state? If yes → class. If only data → dataclass. If neither → a field on something else.

Noun-by-noun evaluation

NounDecisionDeep reasoning — including what breaks if you get it wrong
KeepUser Full class Has identity (unique ID), a name, and email. Appears in every operation — as the payer of an expense, as a participant in a split, as a creditor or debtor in settlement. If User were just a string name, you'd have no way to handle two users with the same name ("There are two Priyhas in this group"). The user_id as the stable identity key is critical for the balance dict keying, which we'll see in the balance tracking section.
KeepGroup Full class Has mutable state (member list, expense list, running balance sheet) and behavior (add member, add expense, compute net balances). Critically, Group is the aggregate — it is the boundary within which balances are computed. Debts exist within a group, not globally. Without Group as a proper class with its own balance state, you have no clear owner for "who owes whom in this trip." The state would scatter into a global dict or into ExpenseManager, both of which make multi-group support impossible.
KeepExpense Full class Records what happened: who paid, how much, the description, and the resulting splits. It is an immutable fact — once added, it should not change (changing it retroactively corrupts historical balances). Has an ID, a timestamp (for auditing), and owns a list of Splits. Without Expense as a class, there is no clean place to record the payer and description alongside the splits.
KeepSplit Abstract class + subclasses This is the most design-sensitive entity. A Split represents one user's share of one expense. The key question is whether it just stores a pre-computed float or whether it stores the original inputs (percentage, exact amount) and computes on demand. A flat float loses information — you can no longer tell if "₹33.34" came from an equal split or an exact split, which matters for validation and display. The abstract base class with three concrete subclasses (EqualSplit, ExactSplit, PercentSplit) is the right design — explored fully in Section 04.
KeepTransaction Simple dataclass The output of the settlement algorithm. "Alice pays Bob ₹500." It has three fields: payer, payee, amount. No behavior, no mutable state — it is pure read-only output. Using a @dataclass is correct here: lightweight, structured, named fields. Do not make it a full class — it has nothing to enforce.
SkipBalance Not a class — it's a dict Tempting to create a Balance class. Resist it. A balance is a derived value — it is computed from expenses, not something you directly set. The "balance sheet" is a nested dict: dict[user_id, dict[user_id, float]]. Making it a class adds boilerplate with no benefit. The dict is sufficient, and its invariants are enforced by the methods that update it, not by a wrapper class.
SkipSplitType Enum EQUAL, EXACT, PERCENT are labels, not objects. They have no state and no behavior. An Enum gives you type safety (no typos), autocomplete, and exhaustive checking in match statements. A string like "equal" gives you a silent bug when someone types "Equal".
Split vs Transaction — the confusion that breaks interviews

The single most common mistake in Splitwise interviews is conflating these two. A Split is one person's share of one specific expense — it is created when the expense is added, and it lives inside the Expense object. A Transaction is a settlement instruction produced at the end — "to clear all debts, Alice should pay Carol ₹700." One group trip might have 20 expenses with 60 Splits, but the settlement algorithm produces only 3 Transactions. They operate at completely different levels. If you name them interchangeably during your interview, the interviewer will flag it.

Why Group owns the balance sheet, not ExpenseManager

It might seem natural to let ExpenseManager hold all balances centrally. But consider: a user belongs to multiple groups. Their debt to someone in a "Thailand Trip" group is completely separate from their debt in a "Flatmates" group. If ExpenseManager holds one global balance dict, you cannot cleanly separate balances by group — you'd need to key by (group_id, user_id, user_id), a three-level dict that is awkward and error-prone. Putting the balance sheet inside Group means each group's debts are naturally isolated. It also means that if you delete a group, all its balances are deleted with it — no orphaned state.

Step 3 · ~15 minutes
Class Design — Every Line Justified
Design top-down: start with the orchestrator (the single entry point), then the most complex domain object (Group and its balance sheet), then the supporting entities. For every field ask: "what happens if this field doesn't exist?" For every method: "who else would own this if I removed it from here?"

ExpenseManager — the Single Entry Point

In every well-structured LLD there is one orchestrator. For Swiggy it is SwiggySystem. For Splitwise it is ExpenseManager. External code touches only this class. It owns all registries, validates inputs, coordinates entities, and delegates work. It does not implement business rules — it orchestrates them.

ExpenseManager — fields and methods
class ExpenseManager: users: dict[str, User] groups: dict[str, Group] engine: SettlementEngine
Why dict, not list, for both registries? Every operation begins with a lookup: "give me the group with this ID." With a list, that's O(n) — scan all groups until you find the right one. With a dict it's O(1). Since add_expense(), settle_up(), and record_payment() all start with a group lookup, using a list would make every operation O(n) in the number of groups — a silent performance regression that gets worse as groups accumulate.
If you used a list: Each of the 5+ methods that look up a group would scan the entire list. With 10,000 groups, every operation is 10,000 comparisons. The dict lookup is a constant-time key access.
engine: SettlementEngine
Why does the engine live on ExpenseManager, not on Group? Settlement is an algorithm that operates on a group's balance data — it doesn't belong to the group's own lifecycle. Group's job is to maintain correct balances. ExpenseManager's job is to expose operations — including running the settlement algorithm. Putting it on Group would mean Group knows about settlement strategy, which is a cross-cutting concern. Putting it on ExpenseManager means you can swap strategies (greedy heap vs. graph-based) without touching Group.
def add_expense( self, group, paid_by, amount, split_type, participants, **kwargs ) -> Expense
Why does ExpenseManager create the splits, not the caller? If the caller constructed Split objects and passed them in, validation would scatter to every call site. One caller forgets to check that percentages sum to 100. Another passes an empty participants list. By centralising creation in add_expense() (via SplitFactory), there is exactly one place where splits are built and validated. Every caller gets the same guarantees automatically. This is the principle: push validation to the boundary where data enters the system, not to the interior where it's assumed clean.
def settle_up( self, group ) -> list[Transaction]
Why return Transactions instead of printing them? Returning a list of Transaction objects makes the method testable and composable. A test can call settle_up(), inspect the list, and assert exactly which transactions were produced — without capturing stdout. A UI layer can receive the list and render it however it wants. Printing is a side effect that makes both impossible. Never bake presentation into a domain method.
def record_payment( self, group, txn: Transaction ) -> None
Why accept a Transaction object instead of (payer_id, payee_id, amount)? The natural workflow is: call settle_up(), get a list of Transaction objects, let the user pay them one by one, then call record_payment(txn) for each. If you accept primitives instead of a Transaction, the caller must unpack and repack. Accepting the Transaction object makes the API mirror the workflow — the output of settle_up() is directly the input of record_payment(). This reduces cognitive overhead and prevents mismatched parameter ordering bugs (is it payer first or payee first?).

Group — the Central Aggregate

Group is where the most complex state lives. The balance sheet is a nested dict that must be updated atomically with every expense. Every field exists because removing it would break something specific.

Group — fields, balance sheet, methods
group_id: str # UUID name: str members: list[User] expenses: list[Expense] balances: dict[str, dict[str, float]]
Why keep both expenses and balances? Isn't balances redundant? They serve different purposes. expenses is the immutable audit trail — the historical record of what happened. balances is the derived running total — the current state. Recomputing balances from expenses on every query is O(expenses × members) per query — a problem that grows unboundedly. The balance dict is an incremental cache updated on every add_expense() call, making every balance query O(1). The tradeoff: you must maintain the invariant that balances are always consistent with expenses — discussed in Section 05.
balances: dict[str, dict[str, float]] # balances[A][B] = amount B owes A # Positive = A is owed by B # Negative = A owes B
Why this specific sign convention? balances[A][B] answers: "from A's perspective, what is B's debt to A?" A positive value means B owes A money — A is a creditor. A negative value means A owes B money — A is a debtor. This convention makes the settlement algorithm natural: users with a positive net balance are creditors, negative are debtors. The invariant is: balances[A][B] == -balances[B][A] always. Both sides must be updated atomically — if you update only one direction, the invariant breaks and your settlement produces wrong results. This is the most critical correctness constraint in the system.
If you update only one direction: Alice pays ₹900 for dinner. You record balances["alice"]["bob"] += 300 but forget balances["bob"]["alice"] -= 300. Now the settlement sees Alice as owed ₹300 but Bob as owing ₹0. The settlement produces "nobody owes Alice anything." The ₹300 debt has been silently erased. This is undetectable until someone checks the numbers.
def add_expense( self, expense: Expense ) -> None: self.expenses.append(expense) self._update_balances(expense)
Why is add_expense() on Group, not on ExpenseManager? Balance update is Group's own internal state change — it is not a cross-entity coordination task. Group owns balances, so it must own the logic that updates balances. If ExpenseManager updated balances directly, it would be reachng into Group's internals — a violation of encapsulation. The rule: behavior that mutates an object's state should live on that object. ExpenseManager creates the Expense and delegates group.add_expense(expense). Group handles its own state update.
def _update_balances( self, expense ) -> None: payer_id = expense.paid_by .get_id() for split in expense.splits: pid = split.user.get_id() if pid == payer_id: continue amt = split.get_amount() self._credit(payer_id, pid, amt)
Why skip the payer's own split? The payer paid the full expense on behalf of everyone, including themselves. Their share is already paid — they don't owe themselves. If you don't skip the payer, you'd record balances[payer][payer] += X, which is nonsensical and breaks the settlement algorithm (it produces a self-referential transaction: "Alice pays Alice ₹30"). The if pid == payer_id: continue is not a micro-optimisation — it is a correctness requirement.
def get_net_balances( self ) -> dict[str, float]: return { uid: round( sum(d.values()), 2) for uid, d in self.balances.items() }
Why collapse the pairwise dict into a flat net balance? The settlement algorithm doesn't need to know "Alice owes Bob ₹300 and Carol ₹200." It only needs "Alice owes ₹500 in total." The pairwise dict is for display ("show me exactly what I owe each person"). The net balance is for settlement ("compute the minimum transactions to clear all debts"). get_net_balances() collapses the matrix into one number per user — the input the settlement engine needs. The rounding to 2 decimal places is not cosmetic; floating-point arithmetic on many small splits can accumulate errors like ₹0.0000000001 that confuse the algorithm.
Sanity check: The sum of all net balances must always equal zero. If Alice nets +₹500 and Bob nets -₹300 and Carol nets -₹200, that's +500 - 300 - 200 = 0. Any non-zero sum indicates a bug in your balance update logic. Add this as an assertion in your tests.

Expense and Split — immutability by design

Expense — the immutable record
class Expense: expense_id: str # UUID paid_by: User amount: float splits: list[Split] description: str created_at: datetime
Why store paid_by as a User object, not a user_id string? Storing the User object means that when you display "Alice paid ₹900 for dinner," you can call expense.paid_by.get_name() directly. No second lookup required. If you stored only the user_id, you'd need to pass the user registry everywhere to display expenses. The User object is the complete snapshot — name, email, ID — at zero extra cost. This is the principle: store the richest representation you have when it's cheap to do so.
If you stored user_id strings: display_expense(expense, user_registry) — now every method that touches Expense must also accept the registry. The method signature grows. The coupling grows. The test setup grows.
created_at: datetime
Why is created_at important — it seems like a minor addition? It is never minor. Real needs: (1) ordering expenses chronologically in the group's feed; (2) dispute resolution — "I added this expense on March 3rd"; (3) auditing — "show me all expenses from last week"; (4) if you ever add expense editing, you need to know the original timestamp versus the edit timestamp. Without created_at, every one of these either becomes impossible or requires a painful migration. Cost: one field. Benefit: permanent historical accuracy.
Split — abstract base (see Section 04 for subclasses)
class Split(ABC): user: User @abstractmethod def get_amount(self) -> float: ... @abstractmethod def validate(self) -> bool: ...
Why two abstract methods instead of just get_amount()? get_amount() returns how much this user owes — the computation. validate() checks whether the split's own inputs are internally valid — the guard. They are separate concerns. An EqualSplit always validates (amount ≥ 0 is the only check). A PercentSplit validates that its percentage is between 0 and 100. An ExactSplit validates that its amount is non-negative. Cross-split validation (do all percentages sum to 100?) is not the individual split's job — it's the factory's job. Each split only validates itself.
user: User # not user_id
Why store the full User object in Split, not just the user_id? When Group._update_balances() iterates splits, it needs to call split.user.get_id() to update the balance dict. If Split stored only a string ID, _update_balances() would need access to the user registry to look up the User — an unnecessary dependency injection into a method that has nothing to do with user management. The User object carries its own ID: split.user.get_id() is always available with no extra lookups.
Step 4 · Deep Dive
Split Strategies — Bad to Great
The way you model Split determines whether your design is extensible or brittle. There are three levels of thinking here. Each solves a concrete failure the previous level introduces.
Bad One class, one field — store a pre-computed float everywhere

Approach

The "obvious" implementation: before creating any Split, compute each user's amount in add_expense(), then pass the result to a single flat Split class.

class Split: def __init__(self, user, amount: float): self.user = user self.amount = amount # always pre-computed # In add_expense(): if split_type == "EQUAL": share = total / len(participants) splits = [Split(u, share) for u in participants] elif split_type == "PERCENT": splits = [Split(u, (pct/100)*total) for u, pct in percents.items()] elif split_type == "EXACT": splits = [Split(u, amt) for u, amt in exact_amounts.items()]

Why this fails — three separate problems

Problem 1 — Logic scattered into add_expense(): add_expense() now has two responsibilities — coordinating the expense creation AND knowing how to compute each split type. When a fourth split type is needed (say, "Adjustment Split"), a developer opens add_expense() — the most critical method — and adds another elif branch. Every such edit is a risk to existing logic. This is the Open/Closed violation: the class must be modified for every extension.

Problem 2 — No validation home: For a percentage split, you need to verify all percentages sum to 100. For exact amounts, they must sum to the total. With the flat approach, where does this validation live? Inside the elif? That's still inside add_expense(). Now add_expense() has a third responsibility: validating inputs per split type. The method grows unboundedly.

Problem 3 — Lost information: Once you store a flat float, you cannot tell whether "₹33.34" came from an equal split of ₹100 ÷ 3, an exact split, or a 33.34% split. This matters for display ("Alice pays 33%"), for re-validation, and for audit trails. You've thrown away the inputs that produced the number.

The concrete extensibility failure: Your manager asks you to add an "Adjustment Split" type (equal base + per-person adjustment). You open add_expense(). It already has 50 lines. You add another elif block. The PR touches the core orchestrator method. Code review flags it. Tests break on unrelated paths because you changed a shared method. This is what "closed for modification" protects against.
Good Subclass per split type — Strategy Pattern

The insight

Each split type is a different algorithm for computing a user's share. Three algorithms, same interface — that is exactly what the Strategy Pattern is for. Make Split abstract, create a concrete subclass per type. Each subclass owns its own computation logic and its own validation rules.

class EqualSplit(Split): def __init__(self, user, amount: float): self._user = user self._amount = amount # pre-computed equal share def get_amount(self) -> float: return self._amount def validate(self) -> bool: return self._amount >= 0 class PercentSplit(Split): def __init__(self, user, percent: float, total: float): self._user = user self._percent = percent self._amount = round((percent / 100) * total, 2) def get_amount(self) -> float: return self._amount def get_percent(self) -> float: return self._percent # original input preserved def validate(self) -> bool: return 0 < self._percent <= 100 class ExactSplit(Split): def __init__(self, user, amount: float): self._user = user self._amount = amount def get_amount(self) -> float: return self._amount def validate(self) -> bool: return self._amount >= 0

Why this is much better

Each split type owns its own logic. Adding an AdjustmentSplit is a new file with a new class — add_expense() never changes. The original percentage is preserved in PercentSplit._percent — no information is lost. Each subclass's validate() enforces its own per-instance rules.

The remaining problem

add_expense() still has the if/elif block that decides which subclass to instantiate. It has to import all three concrete classes. When you add AdjustmentSplit, you add a new elif to add_expense(). You've moved the problem, but it's still in the orchestrator.

Worse: cross-split validation (do all percentages sum to 100?) has no clean home. You could put it in add_expense(), but now add_expense() is back to having split-type-specific logic. You could add a class method to each subclass like PercentSplit.validate_group(splits), but that's awkward — why would one split validate a list of its siblings?

Great Strategy Pattern + Factory — creation and validation centralised

The insight

Add a SplitFactory. Its job: given a split type and the raw inputs, build and validate the list of Split objects. add_expense() calls the factory and never imports a concrete Split class. The factory has separate methods per type, so each method is small, focused, and independently testable.

class SplitFactory: @staticmethod def create_equal( users: list[User], total: float ) -> list[EqualSplit]: n = len(users) share = round(total / n, 2) splits = [EqualSplit(u, share) for u in users] # Rounding fix: ₹100 / 3 = ₹33.33 × 3 = ₹99.99, not ₹100 # Give the leftover penny to the last person. remainder = round(total - share * n, 2) if remainder: splits[-1]._amount = round( splits[-1]._amount + remainder, 2) return splits @staticmethod def create_exact( user_amounts: dict[User, float], total: float ) -> list[ExactSplit]: # Cross-split validation: all amounts must sum to total actual_sum = sum(user_amounts.values()) if abs(actual_sum - total) > 0.01: raise ValueError( f"Exact amounts {actual_sum:.2f} must equal total {total:.2f}") return [ExactSplit(u, amt) for u, amt in user_amounts.items()] @staticmethod def create_percent( user_percents: dict[User, float], total: float ) -> list[PercentSplit]: # Cross-split validation: all percentages must sum to 100 pct_sum = sum(user_percents.values()) if abs(pct_sum - 100) > 0.01: raise ValueError( f"Percentages sum to {pct_sum}, must be 100") return [PercentSplit(u, pct, total) for u, pct in user_percents.items()]

The rounding fix deserves its own explanation

₹100 ÷ 3 = ₹33.333... Rounded to 2 decimal places: ₹33.33 each. Three people × ₹33.33 = ₹99.99 — a penny short of the actual expense. The penny disappears from the system, and the payer is now ₹0.01 under-compensated. Over thousands of expenses, these rounding errors accumulate.

The fix: compute one share, give everyone that share, then add the difference (₹0.01) to the last person. The total is now exact. This is the same technique banks use for currency rounding in batch payments. It is not a detail — it is a correctness requirement for any system that handles money.

Why > 0.01 tolerance instead of strict equality for validation? Floating-point arithmetic means 33.33 + 33.33 + 33.34 might compute as 99.99999999999998 rather than exactly 100. Strict equality would reject valid inputs due to machine precision errors. The 0.01 tolerance absorbs floating-point noise while still catching genuine user errors (like 40% + 40% + 40% = 120%).
What to say in the interview: "I use a Factory for split creation because it centralises both instantiation and cross-split validation. Each split type's factory method is responsible for one type of input — small, focused, independently testable. add_expense() never imports a concrete Split class. Adding a new split type is one new factory method and one new elif in add_expense() — zero changes to existing logic."
Step 5 · Deep Dive
Balance Tracking — Bad to Great
Every time an expense is added, you need to update the record of who owes whom. The question is: when do you compute it, and how do you store it? These seem like implementation details — they are not. They determine correctness, performance, and the complexity of the settlement algorithm.
Bad Recompute from scratch on every query

Approach

Don't store any balance state. When someone asks "what does Alice owe?", scan every expense, look at every split, sum up the result.

def get_balance(self, group, user): total = 0.0 for expense in group.expenses: if expense.paid_by == user: # others owe you for split in expense.splits: if split.user != user: total += split.get_amount() else: # check if user is a participant for split in expense.splits: if split.user == user: total -= split.get_amount() return total

Why this fails

The code itself is already tricky — notice how easy it is to double-count or miss a case. But the real problem is performance. With E expenses and M members per group, every balance query is O(E × M). In a group with 500 expenses and 20 members, every "what do I owe?" call scans 10,000 split objects.

The settlement algorithm calls get_balance() for every member, making settlement O(M × E × M) = O(M²E). A group trip with 20 people and 200 expenses: 20 × 200 × 20 = 80,000 operations per settlement call. And this gets worse over time — the longer a group runs, the slower every query gets.

The hidden correctness risk: "Recompute from scratch" sounds safe — no stale state. But the logic above is easy to get wrong. What if someone is both the payer and a participant in the same expense? What if an expense has no splits? The more conditional branches in the recomputation, the more edge cases to handle every time someone queries a balance. With incremental update, the logic runs once at add-time and is correct from that point forward.
Good Incremental pairwise balance dict — update on every add_expense()

The insight

Maintain a running balance sheet that you update at write time, not read time. When an expense is added, immediately update the balances. Queries become O(1). The cost moves from "every read" to "every write" — and writes (adding expenses) are far less frequent than reads (checking balances).

def _update_balances(self, expense: Expense) -> None: payer_id = expense.paid_by.get_id() for split in expense.splits: pid = split.user.get_id() if pid == payer_id: continue # payer doesn't owe themselves amt = split.get_amount() # payer is owed `amt` by participant — update both directions self.balances.setdefault(payer_id, {}) self.balances.setdefault(pid, {}) self.balances[payer_id][pid] = ( self.balances[payer_id].get(pid, 0) + amt) self.balances[pid][payer_id] = ( self.balances[pid].get(payer_id, 0) - amt)

The invariant: balances[A][B] == -balances[B][A] must always hold. Both directions are updated in the same method call — there is no window where one side is updated and the other isn't.

The remaining problem

This structure works for O(1) pairwise lookups. But the settlement algorithm doesn't need pairwise data — it needs each user's net balance. Computing the net requires summing all of a user's pairwise entries: O(M) per user, O(M²) for the whole group. Also, the pairwise dict can accumulate many small entries across many expenses. It tells you exactly what Alice owes Bob and what Alice owes Carol — but not Alice's total position.

Great Pairwise dict + net collapse — two views for two purposes

The insight

Keep the pairwise dict for detailed queries ("exactly how much does Alice owe Bob?") — it's the UI-facing view. Add a get_net_balances() method that collapses it into a flat dict[user_id, float] — the algorithm-facing view. Each view is derived from the same source of truth (the pairwise dict) and serves a different consumer.

def get_net_balances(self) -> dict[str, float]: """ Collapse the pairwise matrix into one net number per user. Positive = this user is owed money overall (creditor). Negative = this user owes money overall (debtor). The sum of all net balances always equals zero. """ return { uid: round(sum(d.values()), 2) for uid, d in self.balances.items() } # Example trace — Alice pays ₹900 for dinner, equal 3-way split: # # After _update_balances(): # balances["alice"]["bob"] = +300 (bob owes alice ₹300) # balances["alice"]["carol"] = +300 (carol owes alice ₹300) # balances["bob"]["alice"] = -300 (bob owes alice ₹300) # balances["carol"]["alice"] = -300 (carol owes alice ₹300) # # get_net_balances(): # alice: +300 + +300 = +600 ← owed ₹600 total # bob: -300 = -300 ← owes ₹300 # carol: -300 = -300 ← owes ₹300 # Sum: 600 - 300 - 300 = 0 ✓

Why the two-view design is correct

The settlement algorithm gets exactly what it needs — one number per person — without needing to understand the pairwise structure. The UI gets the pairwise detail it needs to show "you owe Bob ₹300 and Carol ₹0" — without any additional computation. The pairwise dict is the single source of truth that both views derive from.

The zero-sum invariant as a test: In your test suite, after every add_expense() call, add: assert abs(sum(group.get_net_balances().values())) < 0.01. If this ever fails, you have a bug in your balance update logic — either you updated only one direction, or you double-counted a split. This single assertion catches an entire class of balance corruption bugs.
Step 6 · Deep Dive
Settlement Algorithm — Bad to Great
Given a set of net balances — some positive (creditors), some negative (debtors) — produce a list of payment instructions that settles all debts. The goal: minimize the total number of transactions. This is the algorithmically interesting part of the problem.

The problem statement, precisely

After a group trip: Alice nets +₹500 (owed ₹500 overall), Bob nets -₹200 (owes ₹200), Carol nets -₹300 (owes ₹300). We need transactions that bring everyone to zero, using as few payments as possible. The naive answer is Bob pays Alice ₹200 and Carol pays Alice ₹300 — two transactions. But what if Alice nets +₹200, Bob nets -₹200, Carol nets +₹100, Dave nets -₹100? The optimal solution here is two transactions, but a naive approach might produce three.

Bad Pairwise settlement — settle each existing pair directly

Approach

For every pair (A, B) where the balance dict says A owes B, create a transaction directly.

def settle(self, balances: dict) -> list[Transaction]: transactions = [] for uid_a, owed_dict in balances.items(): for uid_b, amount in owed_dict.items(): if amount < -0.01: # uid_a owes uid_b transactions.append( Transaction(uid_a, uid_b, abs(amount))) return transactions

Why this fails

It massively over-counts. Concrete example: Alice owes Bob ₹300, and Bob owes Carol ₹300. Bob nets to zero. The optimal solution is one transaction: Alice pays Carol ₹300 directly. Bob is a pass-through — his money comes in from Alice and goes straight out to Carol. The pairwise approach produces two transactions: Alice → Bob ₹300, then Bob → Carol ₹300.

More generally: with n people, pairwise settlement can produce O(n²) transactions, while the optimal is at most n−1. For 10 people: up to 90 pairwise transactions vs. at most 9 optimal ones. That's a 10× difference in how many payments group members have to make.

Why does the pairwise approach overshoot? Because it looks at bilateral relationships. Alice owes Bob ₹300 AND Bob owes Carol ₹300 are treated as two separate facts. The algorithm never notices that Bob is a neutral party whose debt and credit cancel perfectly. Optimal settlement requires collapsing to net balances first — only then can you route payments efficiently.
Good Net balance + two-list matching

The insight

Pairwise relationships are irrelevant for settlement. What matters is each person's net: positive means they need to receive money (creditor), negative means they need to pay (debtor). Separate into two lists and match them: for each debtor, have them pay the first creditor in the list until one of them reaches zero.

def settle(self, net: dict[str, float]) -> list[Transaction]: creditors = sorted( [(uid, bal) for uid, bal in net.items() if bal > 0.001], key=lambda x: x[1] # ascending by amount owed ) debtors = sorted( [(uid, -bal) for uid, bal in net.items() if bal < -0.001], key=lambda x: x[1] # ascending by amount owed ) txns = [] i, j = 0, 0 while i < len(creditors) and j < len(debtors): c_id, c_amt = creditors[i] d_id, d_amt = debtors[j] settled = min(c_amt, d_amt) txns.append(Transaction(d_id, c_id, round(settled, 2))) creditors[i] = (c_id, c_amt - settled) debtors[j] = (d_id, d_amt - settled) if creditors[i][1] < 0.001: i += 1 if debtors[j][1] < 0.001: j += 1 return txns

Why this is much better

This correctly handles the pass-through case. Bob nets to zero, so he doesn't appear in either list. Alice pays Carol directly. One transaction. Correct and efficient.

The remaining problem

The sorted lists are processed in ascending order — smallest amounts first. This is not optimal for minimizing transaction count. Consider: creditors [₹10, ₹50], debtors [₹10, ₹50]. Ascending matching pairs ₹10 debtor with ₹10 creditor (1 transaction, both done), then ₹50 debtor with ₹50 creditor (1 transaction, both done) — 2 transactions total. But if we matched the largest debtor with the largest creditor first, we'd also get 2 transactions. In this case it doesn't matter. But in cases where amounts don't match perfectly, matching largest-with-largest eliminates parties faster — producing fewer total transactions. The sorted-ascending approach can miss this optimization.

Great Greedy two-heap — always match largest creditor with largest debtor

The insight

To always eliminate the most debt per transaction, always pair the largest creditor with the largest debtor. Use two max-heaps — one for creditors, one for debtors. Each iteration pops the largest of each, settles as much as possible, and pushes remainders back. This is O(n log n) and produces at most n−1 transactions.

import heapq from dataclasses import dataclass @dataclass class Transaction: payer_id: str payee_id: str amount: float def describe(self, users) -> str: payer = users[self.payer_id].get_name() payee = users[self.payee_id].get_name() return f"{payer} → {payee} ₹{self.amount:.2f}" class SettlementEngine: def minimize_transactions( self, net_balances: dict[str, float] ) -> list[Transaction]: # Python's heapq is a min-heap. # To get max-heap behaviour, negate all values. creditors = [] # (-amount, uid) — most owed comes first debtors = [] # (-amount, uid) — largest debt comes first for uid, bal in net_balances.items(): if bal > 0.001: heapq.heappush(creditors, (-bal, uid)) elif bal < -0.001: heapq.heappush(debtors, (bal, uid)) # bal is already negative; heapq pops the most negative # first, which is the largest debt. No negation needed. txns = [] while creditors and debtors: neg_c, c_id = heapq.heappop(creditors) credit = -neg_c neg_d, d_id = heapq.heappop(debtors) debt = -neg_d # convert back to positive magnitude settled = min(credit, debt) txns.append(Transaction(d_id, c_id, round(settled, 2))) if credit - settled > 0.001: heapq.heappush(creditors, (-(credit - settled), c_id)) if debt - settled > 0.001: heapq.heappush(debtors, (-(debt - settled), d_id)) return txns

Why greedy works here — and why it's provably optimal

The greedy choice is safe because money is fungible. "Alice pays Carol ₹300" is equivalent to "Alice pays Bob ₹300 and Bob pays Carol ₹300" — the end state is identical. There is no ordering constraint, no dependency between transactions. Because there are no constraints that could be violated by the greedy choice, always eliminating the largest party first is optimal. This is different from, say, a scheduling problem where order of operations creates dependencies.

Why the heap negation trick for debtors? Balance values for debtors are already negative (e.g. -₹500). Python's min-heap pops the smallest value first. The smallest (most negative) value is -₹500, which is the largest debt. So for the debtor heap, we push the raw negative value — no negation needed — and the heap naturally gives us the largest debtor first. For creditors, values are positive, so we negate them to make the most-owed creditor appear smallest to the min-heap.

Complexity

Time: O(n log n) — each person is pushed and popped at most twice. Space: O(n) — two heaps each of at most n entries. Transaction count: at most n−1, which is provably optimal (you need at least one transaction to clear each debtor, and there are at most n−1 debtors).

Trace through a concrete example

# Three friends — one trip, three expenses # Alice nets: +₹500 (creditor) # Bob nets: -₹200 (debtor) # Carol nets: -₹300 (debtor) # # Initial heaps: # creditors: [(-500, "alice")] # debtors: [(-300, "carol"), (-200, "bob")] # → most negative = -300, so carol pops first # # Iteration 1: # Pop largest creditor: alice owes +500 # Pop largest debtor: carol owes 300 # settled = min(500, 300) = 300 # → Transaction: Carol pays Alice ₹300.00 # Remaining credit: 500 - 300 = 200 → push alice back # Remaining debt: 300 - 300 = 0 → carol is done # # Iteration 2: # Pop largest creditor: alice owes +200 # Pop largest debtor: bob owes 200 # settled = min(200, 200) = 200 # → Transaction: Bob pays Alice ₹200.00 # No remainders — both done. # # Result: 2 transactions. Optimal for 3 people. Sum check: 500-200-300=0 ✓
What to say in the interview: "I compute net balances — collapsing the pairwise dict into one number per person — then run a greedy two-heap algorithm. Always matching the largest creditor with the largest debtor eliminates parties as fast as possible, producing at most n−1 transactions. Time is O(n log n). The greedy choice is provably optimal because money is fungible — there are no ordering constraints that could make a different matching produce fewer transactions."

Design Patterns — Justified, Not Just Named
Name a pattern only when you can say: "Without this pattern, here is the specific problem I would face." Each pattern below includes the concrete problem it solves and code showing the before-vs-after.
1. Strategy Pattern for Split types

The specific problem without it: Three split types need three different algorithms for computing a user's share. Without Strategy, all three algorithms live as if/elif branches inside add_expense(). Adding a fourth type means opening the core orchestrator method. Every edit risks breaking existing split logic. Tests must cover all branches together.

✗ Without Strategy
def add_expense(self, ..., split_type):
    if split_type == "EQUAL":
        share = total / len(users)

                  for u in users]
    elif split_type == "PERCENT":
        splits = [Split(u, pct*total/100)
                  for u, pct in pcts.items()]

Adding AdjustmentSplit = editing add_expense(). Four elif branches. Testing requires exercising all paths together. One change breaks unrelated paths.
✓ With Strategy + Factory
class Split(ABC):
    @abstractmethod
    def get_amount(self) -> float: ...

class EqualSplit(Split): ...

class PercentSplit(Split): ...

# add_expense() calls the factory:

# New type? New class + factory method.

New split type = new class + new factory method. add_expense() is closed for modification. Each split type tested in isolation. Zero risk of cross-contamination.

The key insight: The abstract base class is the contract. Every caller (Group._update_balances, settlement engine) calls split.get_amount(). They don't know if they're talking to an EqualSplit or a PercentSplit. This is polymorphism doing its job — the caller is insulated from the implementation.

2. Factory Pattern for Split creation and validation

The specific problem without it: Each split type has different constructor parameters. EqualSplit needs (user, amount). PercentSplit needs (user, percent, total). ExactSplit needs (user, amount). If callers construct these directly, each call site must know the constructor signature — and must remember to run validation before constructing. One call site forgets to validate. ₹40 + ₹40 + ₹40 = ₹120 gets accepted as an exact split for a ₹100 expense. The balance sheet silently overcounts.

The Factory solution: SplitFactory.create_percent(user_percents, total) validates that percentages sum to 100 before constructing anything. If validation fails, an exception is raised before any Split is created. No caller can bypass this — you can't construct a PercentSplit without going through the factory (by convention and code review, since Python doesn't enforce private constructors). The invariant: if a Split object exists, its inputs were valid.

Why validation lives in the Factory, not in the Split constructor

Individual split constructors can only validate themselves (0 < percent <= 100). Cross-split validation — do all percentages sum to 100? — requires seeing the whole list at once. A constructor receives one split's inputs, not all of them. The factory receives all splits at once, making it the only place where cross-split rules can be enforced. This is the right separation: constructors validate self, factories validate the whole.

3. Facade Pattern ExpenseManager as single entry point

The specific problem without it: Without a facade, a caller who wants to add an expense must know about Group (to call add_expense), SplitFactory (to create splits), and the balance update contract. A caller who wants settlement must know about Group (to get net balances) and SettlementEngine (to run the algorithm). The internal complexity is fully exposed. Changing any internal class — say, refactoring how SplitFactory works — forces changes in every caller.

The Facade solution: ExpenseManager is the only public interface. Callers call manager.add_expense(group, paid_by, amount, split_type, ...). ExpenseManager calls SplitFactory, creates the Expense, calls group.add_expense(). The caller never knows SplitFactory exists. If SplitFactory's API changes tomorrow, only ExpenseManager changes — no callers are affected.

The Facade vs. God Class distinction

A common interview follow-up: "Isn't ExpenseManager just a God class?" The answer: No — a Facade is thin. It delegates to the right entity for each operation. It doesn't implement business rules itself. If ExpenseManager starts accumulating logic (computing splits directly, updating balances directly), it becomes a God class. The test: every method in ExpenseManager should be one or two lines that validate inputs and delegate. If any method is 30 lines, the logic has leaked from the domain objects into the facade.

4. Observer Pattern for notifications on expense added

The specific problem without it: When an expense is added, multiple systems might care: send a push notification, log to an audit trail, update an analytics dashboard. Without Observer, all of this lives inside Group.add_expense(). Group now depends on NotificationService, AuditLogger, and AnalyticsService. Adding WhatsApp notifications means opening Group. Testing Group requires mocking all notification infrastructure. Group has four reasons to change instead of one.

✗ Without Observer
def add_expense(self, expense):

    analytics.track(expense)
Group depends on 3 services. Adding email = editing Group. Testing Group requires mocking all 3. Group changes when any notification service changes.
✓ With Observer
class ExpenseObserver(ABC):

        self, group, expense): ...

SOLID Principles — Each With a Real Violation Scenario
SOLID is not a checklist to recite. Each principle prevents a specific category of real bug. For each: the formal definition, what a violation looks like in this Splitwise design, and the specific decision that upholds it.
S
Single Responsibility Principle
"A class should have one, and only one, reason to change."
Violation in Splitwise: Imagine Group has: add_expense(), compute_equal_split(), compute_percent_split(), minimize_transactions(), send_push_notification(). Now Group changes when: split algorithms change, settlement changes, notification infrastructure changes. Three teams edit the same class. One team's change breaks another's test suite. PR review becomes: "why are you touching settlement logic in a PR about push notifications?"
How this design upholds it: Group has one reason to change — group membership and balance sheet maintenance. SplitFactory changes when split creation logic changes. SettlementEngine changes when the settlement algorithm changes. Each class is edited by one concern, tested in isolation, deployed independently.
O
Open / Closed Principle
"Open for extension, closed for modification."
Violation in Splitwise: A new "Adjustment Split" type is needed. Without OCP, you open add_expense(), add an elif, add validation logic, update tests. The most-tested, most-relied-upon method in the system is modified. Any mistake in the new branch can silently break the existing three split types — they share the same method.
How this design upholds it: Add AdjustmentSplit(Split) — a new class, zero changes to existing code. Add SplitFactory.create_adjustment() — a new method. Add one elif to add_expense(). Existing split logic, existing tests, existing validation: all untouched. Extension by addition.
L
Liskov Substitution Principle
"Subclasses must be substitutable for their base class without breaking the program."
Violation in Splitwise: WeirdSplit(Split) overrides get_amount() to return None when the user is not in the expense. Group._update_balances() calls split.get_amount() and does self.balances[payer_id][pid] += amt. None is not a float — TypeError at runtime. The base class promised a float. The subclass returned None. The entire balance update crashes.
How this design upholds it: Every concrete Split must fulfill the same contract: get_amount() returns a non-negative float. It never returns None. validate() returns True or False — never raises an exception. Any Split can be substituted into _update_balances() without the caller knowing or caring which subclass it is.
I
Interface Segregation Principle
"Clients should not be forced to implement interfaces they don't use."
Violation in Splitwise: A single ISplitwise interface with: add_expense(), minimize_transactions(), get_percent(), validate_group(). EqualSplit implements this interface but must provide a get_percent() that raises NotImplementedError — Equal split has no concept of percent. SettlementEngine implements it but must stub get_percent() and validate_group(). Empty stubs are a code smell: they signal an interface that's too broad for its clients.
How this design upholds it: Split interface has exactly two methods: get_amount() and validate(). Every Split subclass uses both. ExpenseObserver has one method: on_expense_added(). No implementor is forced to stub anything. Each interface is precisely as wide as its narrowest client needs.
D
Dependency Inversion Principle
"High-level modules should not depend on low-level modules. Both should depend on abstractions."
Violation in Splitwise: ExpenseManager.__init__() creates self.engine = GreedyHeapEngine() directly. Now: (1) you cannot test ExpenseManager without running the real greedy heap algorithm; (2) switching to a graph-based algorithm means opening ExpenseManager's constructor; (3) A/B testing two algorithms simultaneously is impossible.
How this design upholds it: ExpenseManager.__init__(engine: SettlementEngine) — the abstraction is injected. In production: ExpenseManager(engine=GreedyHeapEngine()). In tests: ExpenseManager(engine=MockEngine()) — runs in milliseconds, deterministic, no real algorithm executing. The test for "does add_expense() correctly update balances?" never needs to run settlement. Dependency injection is what makes this possible.

The practical impact: Every class in this design can be tested with a mock injected for its dependencies. Group has no external dependencies at all — pure in-memory state management. SplitFactory is a collection of static methods — testable without any instantiation. SettlementEngine takes a dict and returns a list — no side effects, testable with a single function call. DIP is what makes each of these independently testable.

Step 9 · Deep Dive
Trade-offs — Concrete Failures, Not Abstractions
Every design decision is a bet. The wrong choice doesn't fail aesthetically — it fails with a specific, real scenario. For each trade-off: what breaks, why it breaks, and what the correct choice costs.
1. Incremental balance update vs. recompute on query
Concrete failure if you recompute on every query: A popular travel group accumulates 500 expenses over a 2-week trip. 20 members. Every time any member opens the app to check their balance, your code scans 500 × 20 = 10,000 split objects. The settlement screen calls this 20 times (once per member): 200,000 operations per settlement page load. As the group grows older, the app gets slower — a feedback loop that makes the most-engaged groups the most painful to use. The balance was correct an hour ago. It will be correct an hour from now. There is no reason to recompute it from raw data each time.
✓ Chosen: incremental updateBalance is updated at write time (add_expense). Every subsequent read is O(1) — one dict lookup. No matter how many expenses accumulate, query time stays constant. The trade-off: correctness discipline — you must always update both directions of the pairwise dict atomically. Forgetting one direction silently corrupts balances.
Cost: correctness disciplineThe bidirectional update invariant must be enforced by the developer. A single missed update in record_payment() (updating balances when a payment is made) corrupts the sheet. Mitigated by: a single method _credit() that always updates both sides, and a zero-sum assertion in tests.
2. Store payer as User object vs. user_id string in Expense
Concrete failure if you store only user_id strings: You want to display "Alice paid ₹900 for dinner." Your display_expense(expense) function calls expense.paid_by and gets "user_abc123". Now you need the name. So display_expense must also accept a user_registry: dict[str, User] parameter. Every function that touches an Expense now carries the registry. Over time: get_expense_summary(expense, user_registry), format_receipt(expense, user_registry), export_to_csv(expenses, user_registry). The user_registry leaks into every layer of the system as an ambient dependency. Every test must set up the registry.
✓ Chosen: store User objectExpense carries the complete User snapshot. expense.paid_by.get_name() works anywhere, no registry needed. The name is snapshotted at add time — if the user later changes their name, historical expenses still show the correct name at the time they were added.
Cost: object reference, not IDIf User objects are large, Expense objects become heavier. In practice, User has 3 fields. The cost is negligible. In a real system with a database, you'd store both the user_id (for relational integrity) and the name snapshot (for historical accuracy) — but in LLD scope, the object reference is the right choice.
3. Settlement on net balances vs. on pairwise balances
Concrete failure if you settle pairwise: Group trip with 6 people. Each pair has some balance — 15 pairwise relationships. The pairwise algorithm produces up to 15 transactions. The net-balance algorithm collapses these to at most 5. Your users would need to make 3× as many payments. In a real app at scale, this is the difference between Splitwise being useful and being noise. Every unnecessary transaction is friction: the app sends a payment request, the recipient has to confirm, both parties have to reconcile their records. The whole value proposition of Splitwise is minimizing this friction.
✓ Chosen: settle on net balancesNet balances collapse all pairwise relationships into one number per person. The greedy heap then produces at most n−1 transactions — provably optimal. The algorithm doesn't care about "who owes whom" historically — only about the net positions that need to be cleared.
Cost: settlements don't reflect real debtsThe settlement might say "Alice pays Carol ₹700" even though Alice never directly incurred any debt to Carol. Historically, Alice owed Bob ₹400 and Bob owed Carol ₹300. Users might find this confusing. Real Splitwise shows both: the minimum-transaction settlement AND the pairwise breakdown for transparency. In LLD scope, the algorithm is the interesting part.
4. SplitFactory as static methods vs. as an injected instance
Concrete failure with static methods at scale: Your company decides split validation rules should be configurable per group — some groups allow tiny rounding tolerance (₹0.001 slack), others require strict zero tolerance. With static methods, the tolerance is a constant in the source code. Changing it requires a code change and redeployment. More importantly, testing different tolerances requires monkey-patching a class-level constant — fragile, thread-unsafe, and a testing anti-pattern.
✓ For LLD scope: static methodsStatic methods are simple, direct, and have no setup cost. No instantiation, no injection wiring. For an interview, this is the right level of complexity — you demonstrate the Factory pattern without over-engineering into a configurable factory. State the limitation explicitly: "In production I'd make SplitFactory an injected instance so the tolerance is configurable per group."
Production alternative: injected factory instanceSplitFactory(tolerance=0.01) passed into ExpenseManager's constructor. Tolerance is now a parameter, not a constant. Different groups can have different factories. Testing different tolerances requires constructing a different factory — clean, no patching. Cost: extra constructor parameter and wiring. Worth it at production scale.
Step 10 · Interview Simulation
Interview Q&A — Deep Answers
These are the questions that come after you present your design. They test whether you understand your own design or memorized it. Every answer goes deeper than the surface — not just what, but exactly why, and what breaks if you choose differently.
Why do you need both the pairwise balance dict AND a net balance method? Why not just one?

They serve different consumers. The pairwise dict answers UI questions: "show me exactly what I owe each person in this group." Alice owes Bob ₹300 and Carol ₹200 — the app can display these individually. The net balance answers algorithm questions: "run settlement to minimize transactions." The greedy heap doesn't care about pairwise relationships — it only needs "Alice nets +₹500, Bob nets -₹200."

If you only had the pairwise dict and no get_net_balances(), the settlement algorithm would need to sum each user's entries itself on every run — O(M) per user, O(M²) total, every call. If you only had net balances and no pairwise dict, you could run settlement but couldn't display "you owe Bob ₹300 specifically." Two different views derived from the same source of truth: the pairwise dict is the write-optimized storage, and get_net_balances() is the read-optimized projection for the algorithm.

How would you add a new "Adjustment Split" type without breaking existing splits?

Exactly three changes, zero touches to existing code:

1. New class: AdjustmentSplit(Split) — implements get_amount() as equal share plus adjustment, and validate() to check the amount is non-negative.

2. New factory method: SplitFactory.create_adjustment(users, total, adjustments) — validates that adjustments sum to zero (they must, otherwise the total changes), then constructs AdjustmentSplit objects.

3. One new elif in add_expense(): elif split_type == SplitType.ADJUSTMENT: splits = SplitFactory.create_adjustment(...).

EqualSplit, ExactSplit, PercentSplit: untouched. Group._update_balances(): untouched — it calls split.get_amount() polymorphically and doesn't care which subclass. SettlementEngine: untouched — it works on net balances, not on split types. This is the Open/Closed Principle in practice: extension by addition, not modification.

Why is the rounding fix in create_equal() necessary, and where exactly does the error come from?

₹100 ÷ 3 = ₹33.333... Two decimal places: ₹33.33. Three people × ₹33.33 = ₹99.99. The payer paid ₹100 but only ₹99.99 is recorded as owed. The balance sheet is short by ₹0.01 permanently — no transaction will ever settle it because ₹0.01 is below any reasonable zero threshold.

Over thousands of equal splits across a real app, these errors accumulate. Finance teams auditing the platform will find consistent ₹0.01 discrepancies per three-way split. Multiply by 10 million splits: ₹100,000 of "missing" money in the system.

The fix: compute one share (₹33.33), give everyone that amount, then calculate the remainder (₹100 - ₹33.33 × 3 = ₹0.01) and add it to the last person (₹33.34). The last person pays slightly more, but the total is now exactly correct. This is the standard technique used in all financial batch payment systems. You assign the rounding adjustment to one party — typically the last in the list, though some systems pick the largest share or the payer themselves.

Why does the settlement algorithm work on net balances rather than the pairwise balance dict directly?

Because pairwise relationships contain more information than the settlement algorithm needs — and that extra information leads to unnecessary transactions.

Concrete example: Alice owes Bob ₹300 (Bob lent her money last month). Bob owes Carol ₹300 (Bob borrowed from Carol for groceries). Pairwise settlement says: Alice pays Bob ₹300, Bob pays Carol ₹300 — two transactions. Net balances say: Alice nets -₹300, Bob nets ₹0, Carol nets +₹300. Bob is neutral — his debt and credit cancel. Settlement: Alice pays Carol directly ₹300 — one transaction.

The pairwise structure preserves historical context: "Alice owes Bob because of X, Bob owes Carol because of Y." But for settlement, historical context is irrelevant. Money is fungible. The only thing that matters is each person's final position. Collapsing to net balances first lets the algorithm route money optimally without being constrained by who-owes-whom history.

What happens end-to-end when a payment is recorded?

The full sequence when Bob pays Alice ₹200:

1. ExpenseManager.record_payment(group, txn): Validates the group exists. Delegates to group.record_payment(txn). ExpenseManager is the facade — it validates and delegates, it doesn't implement.

2. Group.record_payment(txn): Extracts payer_id, payee_id, amount from the Transaction. Updates both directions of the balance dict: balances[payer_id][payee_id] += amount and balances[payee_id][payer_id] -= amount. This is the exact inverse of _update_balances() — a debt being cleared is the mirror image of a debt being created.

3. Balance verification: After recording, get_net_balances() will reflect the updated state. Bob's net moves closer to zero. Alice's net moves closer to zero. If Bob pays exactly what he owes, both net to zero. The zero-sum invariant still holds.

Key design point: Record_payment does NOT create an Expense. It is not an expense — it is a financial settlement of existing debts. Adding it as an expense would double-count the debt: first when the original expense was added, and again when the payment was recorded. The balance dict handles payments separately from expenses precisely to avoid this.

How would you support multiple currencies without breaking the existing design?

Three additions, zero changes to core logic:

1. Add currency field to Expense: currency: str (or a Currency enum). The expense now knows what currency it was paid in.

2. Define CurrencyConverter interface: class CurrencyConverter(ABC): def convert(self, amount, from_ccy, to_ccy) -> float: ... with a concrete implementation backed by real FX rates.

3. Inject CurrencyConverter into Group: Group.__init__(base_currency: str, converter: CurrencyConverter). In _update_balances(), before updating the dict, convert the split amount to the group's base currency: amt = converter.convert(split.get_amount(), expense.currency, self.base_currency).

The settlement algorithm, SplitFactory, and ExpenseManager don't change at all. They operate on float amounts — they don't care about currencies. The conversion happens exactly once, at the point where amounts enter the balance dict. In tests, inject a MockConverter with fixed rates.

This is Dependency Inversion in action: Group depends on the abstract CurrencyConverter, not on a live FX feed. The live feed is injected from outside. Tests never make network calls.

Why is SplitFactory a separate class rather than static methods on Split?

Two reasons: responsibility and testability.

Responsibility: The Split abstract base class owns one thing: "given that I exist and have valid inputs, how much does my user owe?" That's get_amount() and validate(). Creating a list of Splits, validating cross-split rules (do percentages sum to 100?), and handling edge cases like rounding correction — these are creation concerns, not existence concerns. Putting them on Split would give Split two responsibilities: computing its own amount AND orchestrating the creation of a whole group of sibling splits. That violates SRP.

Testability: With a separate SplitFactory, you can test SplitFactory.create_percent() in complete isolation — no Group, no Expense, no User needed beyond the inputs. If the factory were class methods on Split, tests would need to set up the full class hierarchy. More importantly, if SplitFactory is injected into ExpenseManager (rather than called statically), you can inject a mock factory in tests that returns pre-built splits — isolating the test from split-creation logic entirely.
