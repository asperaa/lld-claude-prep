Splitwise

Medium ~35 minutes

Understanding the Problem

What is Splitwise? Splitwise is an expense-sharing application. A group of friends go on a trip. Someone pays for dinner, someone else books the hotel, a third person pays for taxis. At the end of the trip, instead of a tangle of "you owe me $20, but wait you paid for lunch so..." — Splitwise tells each person exactly how much they owe, and to whom, with as few transactions as possible.

The problem seems simple at first. But it has a surprising amount of depth. Before you write a single class, take a moment to think about what this system actually has to do.

There are really three distinct problems layered inside Splitwise:

  1. How do you divide an expense? Equal split is obvious. But what about "Alice pays 60% because she got the bigger room"? Or "Bob pays exactly $47.50"? There are multiple algorithms here, and the system needs to support all of them.
  2. How do you track what everyone owes? After ten expenses across six people, you need a data structure that can tell you, at any point, exactly who owes whom how much.
  3. How do you settle up? If Alice owes Bob $30 and Bob owes Carol $30, the optimal settlement is Alice pays Carol $30 directly — one transaction instead of two. Computing this optimal settlement is a non-trivial algorithm.

These three problems map cleanly to the three most interesting design decisions you'll need to make. The interview will probe all three, so keep them in mind throughout.

💡 Interview tip: Before asking any clarifying questions, spend thirty seconds doing this kind of decomposition out loud. Saying "I see three distinct problems here — splitting, tracking, and settling — let me ask questions to nail down each one" immediately signals to your interviewer that you're thinking structurally, not just jumping to code.

Requirements

Medium~5 minutes in interview

Clarifying Questions

The prompt you'll be given is usually something like:

💬 "Design a system like Splitwise that allows users to split expenses in a group and settle debts."

That's intentionally vague. Your job now is to turn it into a concrete specification. A good framework: ask about core actions, boundaries, edge cases, and extensions.

Here's how a real conversation might go. In practice this takes 3–5 minutes. Don't rush it — a vague spec will cost you much more time later when you're halfway through a design that doesn't match what the interviewer wanted.

You
What split types do we need to support? I'm thinking at minimum equal split, but real Splitwise also has exact amounts and percentage splits. Should I design for all three?
Interviewer
Yes, support all three — equal, exact, and percentage splits.

Good. This tells you the split logic needs to be extensible — you can't just hardcode one algorithm.

You
When someone adds an expense, is it always within a group? Or can two people split something one-on-one without a group?
Interviewer
Let's keep it group-based for now. Every expense belongs to a group.
You
For settlement — should the system just show who owes whom, or should it compute an optimized plan that minimizes the number of transactions?
Interviewer
Minimize transactions. That's the interesting part.

This is important. "Minimize transactions" tells you there's a real algorithm to implement — not just summing up a list.

You
What about validation? For a percentage split, the percentages must add to 100. For exact amounts, they must sum to the total. Should I enforce that?
Interviewer
Yes, reject invalid inputs with a clear error. Don't let bad data corrupt the system.
You
Do all participants in a split have to be members of the group? Or can an expense involve someone outside the group?
Interviewer
All participants must be group members.
You
Should I worry about persistence, a REST API, authentication, or currency conversion?
Interviewer
No — in-memory is fine. Focus on the object model and algorithms.
⚠️ Don't skip the "what's out of scope" question. In a 35-minute interview, spending 10 minutes designing a REST API or a currency converter means you never get to the interesting parts. Explicitly calling out what you're NOT building shows scope awareness, which is a senior-level signal.

Final Requirements

In Scope

  • Register users and create groups
  • Add an expense: who paid, total amount, split type, and participants
  • Three split types: Equal, Exact, Percentage
  • View the current balance for a user in a group
  • Generate a settlement plan that minimizes the number of transactions
  • Record that a payment was made (marking debts as settled)
  • Validate splits: percentages must sum to 100, exact amounts must sum to total
  • Reject invalid inputs gracefully — don't corrupt state

Out of Scope

  • Persistent storage or database
  • REST API or HTTP layer
  • Authentication and authorization
  • Currency conversion
  • Expense editing or deletion

Core Entities

Medium~5 minutes in interview

Finding the Nouns

The standard approach: scan your requirements for nouns that seem to have state or behavior. Not every noun becomes a class — some are just primitives. The goal here is to identify what genuinely deserves to be modeled as an object.

Let's go through the candidates one by one and decide.

Candidate Does it have state? Does it have behavior? Verdict
User Name, email, ID Not much — mostly a data holder ✓ Entity
Group Members, expenses, running balance sheet Add members, add expenses, compute balances ✓ Entity
Expense Who paid, amount, description, splits It's mostly data, but it owns its splits ✓ Entity
Split User + their share of one expense Compute share (different algorithm per type) ✓ Entity — this is where the Strategy Pattern lives
Transaction Payer, payee, amount None — pure output data ✓ Simple data class (output of settlement)
Floor / Amount Just a number None ✗ Stay as primitives (float, int)

The One That's Easy to Get Wrong: Split vs Transaction

A very common mistake in interviews is conflating Split and Transaction. They are completely different things, and mixing them up will corrupt your design.

Here is the exact distinction:

  • A Split is one person's share of a single expense. It is created when an expense is added. It answers: "Of this $90 dinner, how much does Alice owe?" A Split lives inside an Expense.
  • A Transaction is a settlement instruction produced by the algorithm at the end. It answers: "To settle all debts across all expenses in this group, Alice should pay Bob $40." A Transaction is the output of the settlement step.
⚠️ Think of it this way: one group trip might have 15 expenses with 45 Splits across them — but the settlement algorithm might produce only 3 Transactions to clear everything up. They operate at entirely different levels.

Relationships at a Glance

  • A User can belong to many Groups
  • A Group has many Expenses and many Users
  • An Expense belongs to one Group, was paid by one User, and has many Splits
  • Each Split belongs to one User and one Expense
  • A Transaction is produced on demand by the settlement algorithm — it's not stored inside any other object

Class Design

Medium~10 minutes in interview

Work Top-Down

A useful habit: design from the entry point inward. What does external code touch first? In Splitwise, the entry point is a manager or controller — something that accepts calls like "add this expense" and coordinates the rest. Design that interface first, then drill into each entity it touches.

For each class, ask two questions: What does it need to remember? (state) and What actions does it need to support? (methods). Derive both directly from your requirements, not from intuition.

ExpenseManager

This is the top-level coordinator. External code talks to this class only — it hides everything else. Let's derive its state from requirements.

"Generate settlement plan"It needs a SettlementEngine — but should that live here or inside Group? Here — because settlement is an algorithm that operates on a group's data, not part of the group's own state.

Now the methods. Every public method should map to a concrete user action:

💡 Notice add_expense takes a split_type parameter rather than a pre-built list of splits. This means ExpenseManager is responsible for creating the splits, not the caller. This is intentional — it keeps validation centralized. We'll come back to how splits get created in the next section.

Group

Group is the aggregate at the center of everything. It owns members, expenses, and — critically — the running balance sheet.

group.py — state only
class Group:
    def __init__(self, name: str):
        self._group_id: str = str(uuid.uuid4())[:8]
        self._name: str = name
        self._members: list[User] = []
        self._expenses: list[Expense] = []
        # balances[uid_a][uid_b] = how much uid_b owes uid_a
        # positive = uid_a is owed by uid_b
        # negative = uid_a owes uid_b
        self._balances: dict[str, dict[str, float]] = {}

The balance structure deserves attention. Why a nested dict instead of a flat list? Because you need O(1) lookup: "how much does Alice owe Bob specifically?" A flat list would require scanning. We'll dig into the exact semantics — and the tradeoffs of different approaches — in the Balance Tracking section.

Expense is mostly a data holder. It records what happened: who paid, how much, for what, and how it was split. It doesn't compute anything itself.

Split (Abstract)

Split is the most design-interesting class. It represents one person's share of one expense. The key question is: should Split just store a pre-computed amount, or should it store the inputs and compute the amount on demand?

Split Strategies

MediumImplementation section

The Core Question

When you add a split to an expense, what exactly does the Split object store? This seems like a small decision, but it has real consequences for extensibility and correctness.

Let's work through three approaches, from naive to correct.

Bad Just store computed amounts everywhere ▶

Approach

The simplest idea: before creating any Split objects, compute the amount each person owes in add_expense(), then pass those amounts directly to a single Split class.

naive approach
class Split:
    def __init__(self, user: User, amount: float):
        self._user = user
        self._amount = amount  # always pre-computed

    def get_amount(self) -> float:
        return self._amount

# In ExpenseManager.add_expense():
if split_type == "EQUAL":
    share = total / len(participants)
    splits = [Split(u, share) for u in participants]
elif split_type == "PERCENT":
    splits = [Split(u, (pct/100) * total) for u, pct in percents.items()]
elif split_type == "EXACT":
    splits = [Split(u, amt) for u, amt in exact_amounts.items()]

Why This Fails

The logic for how to divide an expense is scattered across add_expense() in a chain of if/elif blocks. When you need to add a fourth split type — say, an "Adjustment Split" — you open ExpenseManager and add another elif. Then when you add a fifth type, you open it again. This is the classic Open/Closed violation.

More subtly: add_expense() now has two responsibilities — deciding how to split, and creating the expense. Those are separate concerns. If the split logic has a bug, you have to find it in a method that's also doing five other things.

Also: there's nowhere natural to put validation. With equal split you don't need it. With percentage split you need to check they sum to 100. With exact amounts you need to check they sum to the total. Stuffing all that into add_expense makes it a mess.

Good Subclass per split type (Strategy Pattern) ▶

The Insight

Each split type is a different algorithm for computing a user's share. Different algorithms on the same interface — that's exactly what the Strategy Pattern is for. Make Split abstract, and create a concrete subclass for each type.

splits.py
class EqualSplit(Split):
    """
    Each person pays total / num_participants.
    The amount is pre-computed and passed in.
    validate() just checks it's non-negative.
    """
    def __init__(self, user: User, amount: float):
        super().__init__(user)
        self._amount = amount

    def get_amount(self) -> float:
        return self._amount

    def validate(self) -> bool:
        return self._amount >= 0


class ExactSplit(Split):
    """
    Caller specifies the exact dollar amount.
    This class doesn't validate that all exact splits sum to total —
    that's a cross-split concern handled in the factory.
    """
    def __init__(self, user: User, amount: float):
        super().__init__(user)
        self._amount = amount

    def get_amount(self) -> float:
        return self._amount

    def validate(self) -> bool:
        return self._amount >= 0


class PercentSplit(Split):
    """
    Caller specifies a percentage. Amount is derived from the total.
    This class stores the percentage so we can re-validate later.
    """
    def __init__(self, user: User, percent: float, total: float):
        super().__init__(user)
        self._percent = percent
        self._amount = round((percent / 100) * total, 2)

    def get_amount(self) -> float:
        return self._amount

    def get_percent(self) -> float:
        return self._percent

    def validate(self) -> bool:
        return 0 < self._percent <= 100

Now each split type owns its own logic. Adding an AdjustmentSplit means adding a new class — it doesn't touch any existing code. That's the Open/Closed Principle in action.

The Remaining Problem

We still have a question: who creates these split objects? If add_expense() still has the if/elif block to pick which subclass to instantiate, we've moved the problem but not solved it. add_expense() still has to know about every split type, just to construct them.

Great Strategy Pattern + Factory for creation ▶

The Insight

Add a SplitFactory to centralize creation. add_expense() calls the factory with a split type; the factory handles which subclass to build, how to validate the inputs, and how to handle rounding edge cases. add_expense() never imports EqualSplit, ExactSplit, or PercentSplit directly.

split_factory.pyFactory Pattern
class SplitFactory:
    """
    Centralizes creation of Split objects.
    Each method handles a specific split type's construction and validation.
    ExpenseManager calls these; it never imports concrete Split subclasses.
    """

    @staticmethod
    def create_equal(users: list[User], total: float) -> list[EqualSplit]:
        n = len(users)
        share = round(total / n, 2)
        splits = [EqualSplit(u, share) for u in users]

        # Handle rounding: e.g. $10 / 3 = $3.33 + $3.33 + $3.34
        # The last person absorbs the rounding difference.
        remainder = round(total - share * n, 2)
        if remainder:
            splits[-1]._amount = round(splits[-1]._amount + remainder, 2)
        return splits

    @staticmethod
    def create_exact(
        user_amounts: dict[User, float], total: float
    ) -> list[ExactSplit]:
        # Cross-split validation: all amounts must sum to total
        if abs(sum(user_amounts.values()) - total) > 0.01:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Exact amounts sum to {sum(user_amounts.values()):.2f}, "
                f"but total is {total:.2f}"
            )
        return [ExactSplit(u, amt) for u, amt in user_amounts.items()]

    @staticmethod
    def create_percent(
        user_percents: dict[User, float], total: float
    ) -> list[PercentSplit]:
        # Cross-split validation: all percentages must sum to 100
        if abs(sum(user_percents.values()) - 100) > 0.01:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Percentages sum to {sum(user_percents.values())}, must be 100"
            )
        return [PercentSplit(u, pct, total) for u, pct in user_percents.items()]
💡 Notice the rounding fix in create_equal. $10 / 3 = $3.333... — if you just round each share independently, you get $3.33 + $3.33 + $3.33 = $9.99, which is a penny short. The fix: compute one share, give everyone that amount, then assign the leftover (total − share × n) to the last person. This is a common detail interviewers love to probe.

How This All Wires Together

Now add_expense() becomes a clean delegation chain:

expense_manager.py — add_expense
def add_expense(self, group, paid_by, amount,
                split_type, participants=None,
                exact_amounts=None, percents=None,
                description="") -> Expense:

    members = participants or group.get_members()

    if split_type == SplitType.EQUAL:
        splits = SplitFactory.create_equal(members, amount)

    elif split_type == SplitType.EXACT:
        if not exact_amounts:
            raise ValueError("Exact split requires exact_amounts")
        splits = SplitFactory.create_exact(exact_amounts, amount)

    elif split_type == SplitType.PERCENT:
        if not percents:
            raise ValueError("Percent split requires percents")
        splits = SplitFactory.create_percent(percents, amount)

    else:
        raise ValueError(f"Unknown split type: {split_type}")

    expense = Expense(paid_by, amount, splits, description)
    group.add_expense(expense)
    return expense

Adding a new split type means: (1) add a new Split subclass, (2) add a new factory method, (3) add one elif branch here. Zero changes to any existing class's logic. This is extensibility done right.

Tracking Balances

MediumImplementation section

The Core Question

Every time an expense is added, you need to update the record of who owes whom. The question is: how do you store that information, and when do you compute it?

There are fundamentally different philosophies here, and the difference matters.

Bad Recompute from scratch every time ▶

Approach

Don't maintain any balance state at all. When someone asks "what does Alice owe?", scan every expense in the group, look at every split, and compute the answer on the fly.

recompute approach
def get_balance(self, group: Group, user: User) -> float:
    total = 0.0
    for expense in group.get_expenses():
        for split in expense.get_splits():
            if split.get_user() == user:
                if expense.get_paid_by() == user:
                    pass  # you paid, you don't owe yourself
                else:
                    total -= split.get_amount()  # you owe this

        if expense.get_paid_by() == user:
            for split in expense.get_splits():
                if split.get_user() != user:
                    total += split.get_amount()  # others owe you
    return total

Why This Fails

The code above is already tricky to get right (notice how easy it is to double-count). But the real problem is performance. With E expenses and M members per group, every balance query is O(E × M). In a group with 500 expenses and 20 members, every "who owes what" call scans 10,000 split objects. This gets worse over time — the longer a group runs, the slower every query gets.

And since the settlement algorithm calls get_balance() for every user, settlement degrades to O(M × E × M) = O(M² × E).

Good Incremental pairwise balance dict ▶

The Insight

Maintain a running balance sheet that you update incrementally with every new expense. When an expense is added, immediately update the balances — don't wait to compute them later. Queries become O(1).

The structure is a nested dict: balances[user_a][user_b] = the net amount user_b owes user_a. Positive means user_a is owed that amount by user_b. Negative means user_a owes user_b.

group.py — _update_balances
def _update_balances(self, expense: Expense) -> None:
    payer_id = expense.get_paid_by().get_user_id()

    for split in expense.get_splits():
        participant_id = split.get_user().get_user_id()

        if participant_id == payer_id:
            continue  # you don't owe yourself

        amt = split.get_amount()

        # payer is owed `amt` by participant
        self._balances.setdefault(payer_id, {})
        self._balances[payer_id][participant_id] = (
            self._balances[payer_id].get(participant_id, 0) + amt
        )

        # participant owes payer `amt` (mirror entry)
        self._balances.setdefault(participant_id, {})
        self._balances[participant_id][payer_id] = (
            self._balances[participant_id].get(payer_id, 0) - amt
        )
🔑 The invariant you must maintain: balances[A][B] is always the exact negative of balances[B][A]. If Alice is owed $30 by Bob, then balances["alice"]["bob"] = +30 and balances["bob"]["alice"] = -30. You update both directions atomically in every operation. If you ever update only one side, your balance sheet is inconsistent.

The Remaining Problem

This structure works great for O(1) pairwise lookups. But the settlement algorithm doesn't need pairwise data — it needs each user's net balance (how much they're owed or owe in total). With the pairwise structure, computing that net requires summing across all entries for a user. Also, after many expenses, the pairwise dict can have many small redundant entries (Alice owes Bob $10, Alice owes Carol $5, Alice owes Dave $2...) that don't simplify into a compact representation.

Great Pairwise dict + net balance collapse ▶

The Insight

Keep the pairwise dict for detailed queries ("exactly how much does Alice owe Bob?"), but add a get_net_balances() method that collapses it into a flat dict[user_id, float] for the settlement algorithm. The settlement algorithm only needs net balances — it doesn't care about individual pairwise relationships.

group.py — get_net_balances
def get_net_balances(self) -> dict[str, float]:
    """
    Collapse the pairwise balance matrix into a single net number per user.
    Positive = this user is owed money overall.
    Negative = this user owes money overall.
    """
    net: dict[str, float] = {}
    for uid, owed_by_others in self._balances.items():
        net[uid] = round(sum(owed_by_others.values()), 2)
    return net

Let's trace through a concrete example to make this concrete. Alice pays $90 for dinner. The equal split gives $30 each to Alice, Bob, Carol. Since Alice paid, only Bob and Carol have balances to update.

# After Alice pays $90 for dinner (equal 3-way split):

balances["alice"]["bob"] = +30.0 # alice is owed $30 by bob balances["alice"]["carol"] = +30.0 # alice is owed $30 by carol balances["bob"]["alice"] = -30.0 # bob owes alice $30 balances["carol"]["alice"] = -30.0 # carol owes alice $30

# get_net_balances() collapses this to: net["alice"] = +60.0 # (+30 + +30) net["bob"] = -30.0 # (-30) net["carol"] = -30.0 # (-30)

Now Bob pays $60 for the hotel. Equal split: $20 each.

# After Bob pays $60 for hotel (equal 3-way split):

balances["bob"]["alice"] += +20.0 # → was -30, now -10 balances["bob"]["carol"] = +20.0 # bob is owed $20 by carol balances["alice"]["bob"] += -20.0 # → was +30, now +10 balances["carol"]["bob"] = -20.0 # carol owes bob $20

# get_net_balances() now gives: net["alice"] = +10.0 # (+10 from bob - 0 from carol... wait, let's recheck) net["bob"] = +10.0 # (-10 alice + 20 carol = +10 net) net["carol"] = -20.0 # (-30 alice - 20 bob + 30 = -20 net)

# Note: net balances always sum to zero across all users — a useful sanity check.
💡 Sanity check trick: The sum of all net balances in a group must always equal zero. If it doesn't, you have a bug in your update logic. You can add an assertion in your tests: assert abs(sum(net.values())) < 0.01.

Settlement Algorithm

MediumThe hardest part

The Problem Statement

Given a set of net balances — some people are owed money (creditors), some owe money (debtors) — produce a list of transactions that settles all debts while minimizing the total number of transactions.

Why does minimizing transactions matter? In a group of 6 people, a naive approach might produce 15 transactions ("Alice pays Bob, Alice pays Carol, Bob pays Dave..."). The optimal settlement might need only 5. Fewer transactions means less friction and less cognitive load for the group.

Let's think through how we'd approach this before looking at the algorithm.

Bad Pairwise settlement — settle each pair directly ▶

Approach

For every pair (A, B) in the group, if A owes B anything, create a transaction for it. This is what most people jump to first.

naive settlement
def naive_settle(balances: dict[str, dict[str, float]]) -> list[Transaction]:
    transactions = []
    for uid_a, owed_dict in balances.items():
        for uid_b, amount in owed_dict.items():
            if amount < -0.01:  # uid_a owes uid_b
                transactions.append(Transaction(uid_a, uid_b, abs(amount)))
    return transactions

Why This Fails

It massively over-counts. Consider: Alice owes Bob $30, and Bob owes Carol $30. The pairwise approach creates two transactions: Alice→Bob $30, and Bob→Carol $30. But the optimal solution is one transaction: Alice→Carol $30. Alice's $30 "passes through" Bob, who ends up net zero. The pairwise approach doesn't collapse these chains.

More generally, pairwise settlement creates O(M²) transactions in the worst case for M members. The optimal is at most O(M−1).

Good Net balance + two-pointer matching ▶

The Core Insight

The pairwise structure is irrelevant for settlement. What matters is each person's net balance. If Alice's net is +$60 (she's owed $60 overall) and Bob's net is −$60 (he owes $60 overall), it doesn't matter who specifically owes whom — Bob should just pay Alice $60 directly.

So the algorithm is: separate users into creditors (net positive) and debtors (net negative), then match them up.

two-pointer approach
def settle_two_pointer(net: dict[str, float]) -> list[Transaction]:
    # Split into creditors (owed money) and debtors (owe money)
    creditors = sorted(
        [(uid, bal) for uid, bal in net.items() if bal > 0.001],
        key=lambda x: x[1]
    )
    debtors = sorted(
        [(uid, abs(bal)) for uid, bal in net.items() if bal < -0.001],
        key=lambda x: x[1]
    )

    transactions = []
    i, j = 0, 0

    while i < len(creditors) and j < len(debtors):
        c_id, c_amt = creditors[i]
        d_id, d_amt = debtors[j]

        settled = min(c_amt, d_amt)
        transactions.append(Transaction(d_id, c_id, round(settled, 2)))

        creditors[i] = (c_id, c_amt - settled)
        debtors[j]   = (d_id, d_amt - settled)

        if creditors[i][1] < 0.001: i += 1
        if debtors[j][1]   < 0.001: j += 1

    return transactions

The Remaining Problem

This is correct and much better than pairwise. But with sorted lists, when we process each creditor-debtor pair we pop the next item in sorted order — not necessarily the largest. Consider: creditor of $10 and debtor of $10 would ideally be matched directly (one transaction). But if our sorted list pairs them with others first, we might generate more transactions than needed.

To always minimize transactions, we should always pair the largest creditor with the largest debtor. That way, when one side runs out (say the debtor can fully pay off the creditor), we eliminate one participant entirely, which minimizes the transaction count.

Great Greedy two-heap algorithm ▶

The Insight

To always match largest-with-largest, use a max-heap (priority queue) for both creditors and debtors. Python's heapq is a min-heap, so we negate values to simulate a max-heap. Each iteration: pop the largest creditor and largest debtor, settle the minimum of (what creditor is owed, what debtor owes), push back the remainder if any.

settlement_engine.pyGreedy + Heap
import heapq
from dataclasses import dataclass

@dataclass
class Transaction:
    payer_id: str
    payee_id: str
    amount: float

    def describe(self, users: dict) -> str:
        payer = users[self.payer_id].get_name()
        payee = users[self.payee_id].get_name()
        return f"{payer} → {payee}  ${self.amount:.2f}"


class SettlementEngine:
    """
    Greedy two-heap algorithm.
    Always matches the largest debtor with the largest creditor.
    Produces at most (n-1) transactions for n participants.
    Time: O(n log n). Space: O(n).
    """

    def minimize_transactions(
        self, net_balances: dict[str, float]
    ) -> list[Transaction]:

        # Max-heap for creditors: negate amounts because heapq is a min-heap
        creditors: list[tuple[float, str]] = []
        # Max-heap for debtors: store debt as positive, negate for heap order
        debtors: list[tuple[float, str]] = []

        for uid, balance in net_balances.items():
            if balance > 0.001:        # creditor: owed money
                heapq.heappush(creditors, (-balance, uid))
            elif balance < -0.001:     # debtor: owes money
                heapq.heappush(debtors, (balance, uid))
                # Note: balance is already negative here.
                # The most negative value = largest debt, and heapq.heappop
                # returns the smallest tuple value, so -50 comes before -30.
                # This naturally gives us the largest debtor first.

        transactions = []

        while creditors and debtors:
            neg_credit, creditor_id = heapq.heappop(creditors)
            credit = -neg_credit           # convert back to positive

            debt_neg, debtor_id = heapq.heappop(debtors)
            debt = -debt_neg              # convert to positive magnitude

            # The debtor pays the creditor the smaller of the two amounts
            settled = min(credit, debt)
            transactions.append(Transaction(
                payer_id=debtor_id,
                payee_id=creditor_id,
                amount=round(settled, 2)
            ))

            remaining_credit = credit - settled
            remaining_debt   = debt   - settled

            # Push back whoever still has a remainder
            if remaining_credit > 0.001:
                heapq.heappush(creditors, (-remaining_credit, creditor_id))
            if remaining_debt > 0.001:
                heapq.heappush(debtors, (-remaining_debt, debtor_id))

        return transactions

Why Greedy Works Here

The greedy choice is safe because money is fungible. Paying off the largest creditor first doesn't "block" you from paying smaller creditors later — the amounts can always be split arbitrarily. This is different from, say, a scheduling problem where order matters. Here, the only objective is minimizing the count of transactions, and always eliminating the largest party first is provably optimal for that goal.

Complexity

  • Time: O(n log n) — each person is pushed and popped at most twice from a heap
  • Space: O(n) — two heaps, each holding at most n entries
  • Transaction count: At most n−1 transactions for n people — which is optimal (you need at least one transaction per debtor)
🧠 Why the heap negation trick? Python's heapq always pops the smallest element. We want the largest creditor. So we store amounts as negatives: -50 is "smaller" than -30, so it gets popped first — which corresponds to the $50 creditor, the largest one. This is standard Python max-heap idiom and interviewers know it, so mentioning it shows fluency.

Scenario: Three Friends, Three Expenses

Alice, Bob, and Carol are splitting costs for a weekend trip.

  • Alice pays $90 for dinner — equal split among all three ($30 each)
  • Bob pays $60 for the hotel — equal split ($20 each)
  • Carol pays $100 for tours — percentage split: Alice 50%, Bob 30%, Carol 20%

Step 1: Track balances after each expense

After Expense 1 (Alice pays $90, equal split):

Bob and Carol each owe Alice $30.

balances["alice"]["bob"] = +30 balances["alice"]["carol"] = +30 balances["bob"]["alice"] = -30 balances["carol"]["alice"] = -30

After Expense 2 (Bob pays $60, equal split):

Alice and Carol each owe Bob $20.

balances["alice"]["bob"] += -20 → now +10 (alice is still owed $10 net from bob) balances["bob"]["alice"] += +20 → now -10 (bob now only owes alice $10 net) balances["bob"]["carol"] = +20 balances["carol"]["bob"] = -20

After Expense 3 (Carol pays $100, percent split: Alice 50%, Bob 30%, Carol 20%):

Alice owes Carol $50, Bob owes Carol $30, Carol's own share is $20.

balances["carol"]["alice"] += +50 → now +20 (carol is owed $20 net from alice: -30+50) balances["alice"]["carol"] += -50 → now -20 balances["carol"]["bob"] += +30 → now +10 balances["bob"]["carol"] += -30 → now -50

Step 2: Compute net balances

net["alice"] = balances["alice"]["bob"] + balances["alice"]["carol"] = +10 + (-20) = -10 (alice owes $10 net)

net["bob"] = balances["bob"]["alice"] + balances["bob"]["carol"] = (-10) + (-50) = -60 (bob owes $60 net)

net["carol"] = balances["carol"]["alice"] + balances["carol"]["bob"] = +20 + +10 = +70 (carol is owed $70 net)

Sanity check: -10 + (-60) + 70 = 0 ✓

Step 3: Run the settlement algorithm

Initial state: creditors heap: [(-70, "carol")] debtors heap: [(-60, "bob"), (-10, "alice")] # Remember: most-negative = largest debtor, so bob comes first

Iteration 1: Pop largest creditor: carol owes +70 Pop largest debtor: bob owes 60 settled = min(70, 60) = 60 → Transaction: Bob pays Carol $60.00 Remaining credit: 70 - 60 = 10 → push carol back: (-10, "carol") Remaining debt: 60 - 60 = 0 → bob is done

Iteration 2: Pop largest creditor: carol owes +10 Pop largest debtor: alice owes 10 settled = min(10, 10) = 10 → Transaction: Alice pays Carol $10.00 No remainders — both done.

Result: 2 transactions (optimal for 3 people) Bob → Carol $60.00 Alice → Carol $10.00
💡 During the interview: After implementing the settlement algorithm, do exactly this walkthrough. Say "let me trace through a small example to verify." This is a concrete demonstration of engineering discipline — you don't just write code and assume it works. Interviewers at all levels respond well to this.

from __future__ import annotations
from abc import ABC, abstractmethod
from models import User


class Split(ABC):
    def __init__(self, user: User) -> None:
        self._user = user

    def get_user(self) -> User: return self._user

    @abstractmethod
    def get_amount(self) -> float: ...

    @abstractmethod
    def validate(self) -> bool: ...


class EqualSplit(Split):
    def __init__(self, user: User, amount: float) -> None:
        super().__init__(user)
        self._amount = amount

    def get_amount(self) -> float: return self._amount
    def validate(self) -> bool:    return self._amount >= 0


class ExactSplit(Split):
    def __init__(self, user: User, amount: float) -> None:
        super().__init__(user)
        self._amount = amount

    def get_amount(self) -> float: return self._amount
    def validate(self) -> bool:    return self._amount >= 0


class PercentSplit(Split):
    def __init__(self, user: User, percent: float, total: float) -> None:
        super().__init__(user)
        self._percent = percent
        self._amount  = round((percent / 100) * total, 2)

    def get_amount(self)  -> float: return self._amount
    def get_percent(self) -> float: return self._percent
    def validate(self)    -> bool:  return 0 < self._percent <= 100
split_factory.pyFactory Pattern
from __future__ import annotations
from models import User
from splits import EqualSplit, ExactSplit, PercentSplit


class SplitFactory:
    @staticmethod
    def create_equal(users: list[User], total: float) -> list[EqualSplit]:
        n     = len(users)
        share = round(total / n, 2)
        splits = [EqualSplit(u, share) for u in users]
        # Give rounding remainder to the last person
        remainder = round(total - share * n, 2)
        if remainder:
            splits[-1]._amount = round(splits[-1]._amount + remainder, 2)
        return splits

    @staticmethod
    def create_exact(
        user_amounts: dict[User, float], total: float
    ) -> list[ExactSplit]:
        if abs(sum(user_amounts.values()) - total) > 0.01:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Exact amounts {sum(user_amounts.values()):.2f} "
                f"must equal total {total:.2f}"
            )
        return [ExactSplit(u, amt) for u, amt in user_amounts.items()]

    @staticmethod
    def create_percent(
        user_percents: dict[User, float], total: float
    ) -> list[PercentSplit]:
        if abs(sum(user_percents.values()) - 100) > 0.01:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Percentages sum to {sum(user_percents.values())}, must be 100"
            )
        return [PercentSplit(u, pct, total) for u, pct in user_percents.items()]

from __future__ import annotations
import uuid
from abc import ABC, abstractmethod
from models import User, Transaction


class ExpenseObserver(ABC):
    """Observer interface — subscribe to expense events without
    coupling to Group internals."""
    @abstractmethod
    def on_expense_added(self, group_name: str, expense) -> None: ...


class NotificationService(ExpenseObserver):
    def on_expense_added(self, group_name: str, expense) -> None:
        print(f"[NOTIFY] '{group_name}': ${expense.get_amount():.2f} added "
              f"by {expense.get_paid_by().get_name()} — {expense.get_description()}")


class Expense:
    def __init__(self, paid_by, amount, splits, description=""):
        self._expense_id  = str(uuid.uuid4())[:8]
        self._paid_by     = paid_by
        self._amount      = amount
        self._splits      = splits
        self._description = description

    def get_paid_by(self):    return self._paid_by
    def get_amount(self):     return self._amount
    def get_splits(self):     return self._splits
    def get_description(self): return self._description


class Group:
    def __init__(self, name: str) -> None:
        self._group_id = str(uuid.uuid4())[:8]
        self._name     = name
        self._members: list[User] = []
        self._expenses: list[Expense] = []
        self._balances: dict[str, dict[str, float]] = {}
        self._observers: list[ExpenseObserver] = []

    # ── Observer management ─────────────────────────────────
    def add_observer(self, obs: ExpenseObserver) -> None:
        self._observers.append(obs)

    # ── Members ─────────────────────────────────────────────
    def add_member(self, user: User) -> None:
        if user not in self._members:
            self._members.append(user)
            self._balances.setdefault(user.get_user_id(), {})

    def get_members(self) -> list[User]: return list(self._members)

    # ── Expenses ────────────────────────────────────────────
    def add_expense(self, expense: Expense) -> None:
        self._expenses.append(expense)
        self._update_balances(expense)
        for obs in self._observers:
            obs.on_expense_added(self._name, expense)

    def _update_balances(self, expense: Expense) -> None:
        payer_id = expense.get_paid_by().get_user_id()
        for split in expense.get_splits():
            pid = split.get_user().get_user_id()
            if pid == payer_id:
                continue
            amt = split.get_amount()
            # payer is owed `amt` by participant
            self._balances.setdefault(payer_id, {})
            self._balances[payer_id][pid] = (
                self._balances[payer_id].get(pid, 0) + amt
            )
            # participant owes payer `amt`
            self._balances.setdefault(pid, {})
            self._balances[pid][payer_id] = (
                self._balances[pid].get(payer_id, 0) - amt
            )

    def get_net_balances(self) -> dict[str, float]:
        return {
            uid: round(sum(d.values()), 2)
            for uid, d in self._balances.items()
        }

    def record_payment(self, txn: Transaction) -> None:
        """When debtor pays creditor, reduce those balances."""
        p, q, amt = txn.payer_id, txn.payee_id, txn.amount
        self._balances.setdefault(p, {})
        self._balances.setdefault(q, {})
        self._balances[p][q] = self._balances[p].get(q, 0) + amt
        self._balances[q][p] = self._balances[q].get(p, 0) - amt

    def get_group_id(self) -> str: return self._group_id
    def get_name(self) -> str:     return self._name
expense_manager.pyFacade Pattern + Settlement Engine
from __future__ import annotations
import heapq
from models import User, Transaction, SplitType
from splits import SplitFactory
from group import Group, Expense, NotificationService


class SettlementEngine:
    def minimize_transactions(
        self, net_balances: dict[str, float]
    ) -> list[Transaction]:
        creditors: list[tuple[float, str]] = []
        debtors:   list[tuple[float, str]] = []

        for uid, bal in net_balances.items():
            if bal > 0.001:
                heapq.heappush(creditors, (-bal, uid))
            elif bal < -0.001:
                heapq.heappush(debtors, (bal, uid))

        txns = []
        while creditors and debtors:
            neg_c, cid = heapq.heappop(creditors)
            credit = -neg_c
            neg_d, did = heapq.heappop(debtors)
            debt = -neg_d

            settled = min(credit, debt)
            txns.append(Transaction(did, cid, round(settled, 2)))

            if credit - settled > 0.001:
                heapq.heappush(creditors, (-(credit - settled), cid))
            if debt - settled > 0.001:
                heapq.heappush(debtors, (-(debt - settled), did))

        return txns


class ExpenseManager:
    """Facade — single entry point for all Splitwise operations."""

    def __init__(self) -> None:
        self._users:  dict[str, User]  = {}
        self._groups: dict[str, Group] = {}
        self._engine = SettlementEngine()

    def register_user(self, name: str, email: str) -> User:
        u = User(name, email)
        self._users[u.get_user_id()] = u
        return u

    def create_group(self, name: str) -> Group:
        g = Group(name)
        g.add_observer(NotificationService())
        self._groups[g.get_group_id()] = g
        return g

    def add_member(self, group: Group, user: User) -> None:
        group.add_member(user)

    def add_expense(
        self, group, paid_by, amount, split_type,
        participants=None, exact_amounts=None,
        percents=None, description=""
    ) -> Expense:
        members = participants or group.get_members()

        if split_type == SplitType.EQUAL:
            splits = SplitFactory.create_equal(members, amount)
        elif split_type == SplitType.EXACT:
            if not exact_amounts:
                raise ValueError("Exact split requires exact_amounts")
            splits = SplitFactory.create_exact(exact_amounts, amount)
        elif split_type == SplitType.PERCENT:
            if not percents:
                raise ValueError("Percent split requires percents")
            splits = SplitFactory.create_percent(percents, amount)
        else:
            raise ValueError(f"Unknown split type: {split_type}")

        expense = Expense(paid_by, amount, splits, description)
        group.add_expense(expense)
        return expense

    def get_balances(self, group: Group) -> dict[str, float]:
        return group.get_net_balances()

    def settle_up(self, group: Group) -> list[Transaction]:
        return self._engine.minimize_transactions(
            group.get_net_balances()
        )

    def print_settlement(self, group: Group) -> None:
        txns = self.settle_up(group)
        print(f"\n── Settlement: '{group.get_name()}' ──")
        if not txns:
            print("  Already settled!")
            return
        for t in txns:
            print(f"  {t.describe(self._users)}")

    def record_payment(self, group: Group, txn: Transaction) -> None:
        group.record_payment(txn)

Extensibility

MediumSenior-level follow-ups

If time allows, interviewers add small twists to test whether your design can evolve without surgery. You typically don't need to fully implement these — explaining where the change lands and what it touches is enough.

"How would you add a new split type, like Adjustment Split?"

An Adjustment Split works like equal split, but each person gets an adjustment added or subtracted from their base share. (e.g., $100 split equally is $33.33 each, but Alice gets -$5 adjustment = $28.33, Bob gets +$5 = $38.34.)

"I'd add an AdjustmentSplit class that extends Split and implements get_amount(). Then I'd add a SplitFactory.create_adjustment() method that validates adjustments sum to zero (so they don't change the total), and one new elif branch in add_expense(). No existing class changes. This is the Open/Closed Principle — the system is open for extension but closed for modification."

Key phrase to use: "no existing class changes." That's what the interviewer is listening for.

"How would you support multiple currencies?"

"I'd add a Currency field to Expense. I'd define a CurrencyConverter abstract class with a convert(amount, from_ccy, to_ccy) method and inject it into SettlementEngine. When computing net balances, I'd convert everything to a base currency before summing. In tests, I'd inject a mock converter with fixed rates. The key is that SettlementEngine depends on the abstract converter interface, not on a live FX feed — that's Dependency Inversion."

"What if you wanted different settlement strategies — e.g., one that minimizes the total amount transferred vs. one that minimizes transaction count?"

"I'd extract a SettlementStrategy abstract class with a single settle(net_balances) method. SettlementEngine would hold a reference to a strategy and delegate to it. ExpenseManager's constructor would accept a strategy to inject. Swapping from greedy-heap to a different algorithm means passing a different strategy — nothing else changes. That's Strategy Pattern applied to the settlement algorithm itself."

"How would you handle notifications without coupling Group to any specific notification service?"

"That's the Observer Pattern, which I already have in the design. Group has an add_observer() method that accepts anything implementing ExpenseObserver. It fires on_expense_added() on all registered observers. Group doesn't import NotificationService or AuditLogger directly — it only knows about the interface. So adding a new subscriber is just registering a new observer; Group never changes."

What Each Level Needs

MediumKnow where you stand

Here's what interviewers typically expect at each level for this problem. Use this to calibrate your preparation — don't just aim to get the answer right, aim to get it right in the way that matches the level you're targeting.

Junior Core entities + basic splitting

At the junior level, the expectation is that you can identify the key entities and model basic splitting behavior. Hints are acceptable.

  • Identify User, Group, Expense, and some form of split representation
  • Implement equal split correctly, even if hardcoded — the extensibility to other types can come with hints
  • Maintain some form of balance tracking, even if it's recomputed from scratch each time (O(E×M) is okay here)
  • Be able to explain the difference between a Split and a Transaction when asked
  • Settlement can be simple — pairwise or even just "show balances". The greedy algorithm is a stretch goal

If you can build a working demo where expenses are added, balances are visible, and settlements are shown (even naively), that meets the bar.

Mid-Level Strategy Pattern + correct settlement

At mid-level, extensibility and correctness become the focus. The design should be clean enough that adding a fourth split type doesn't require touching existing code.

  • Implement the Strategy Pattern for splits — abstract Split base class with concrete subclasses — without being prompted
  • Recognize that a factory or creation helper is needed to centralize validation (percentages sum to 100, exact amounts sum to total)
  • Implement incremental balance tracking (the nested dict updated on every add_expense()) rather than recomputing every time
  • Implement the settlement algorithm correctly — at minimum the two-pointer net-balance approach. The greedy heap is a plus.
  • Handle the rounding edge case in equal split ($10/3 problem) either unprompted or immediately when the interviewer hints at it
  • Explain at least one design pattern decision and why it was chosen — not just what it is

The key signal is: when the interviewer asks "how would you add a percentage split?", you should be able to answer without restructuring your existing design.

Senior Every decision explained + extensibility fluency

Senior candidates should produce a design where every significant decision is justified, tradeoffs are stated openly, and follow-up extensions require no surgery to the core design.

  • Drive the clarifying questions conversation — don't wait to be asked. Bring up validation, settlement optimization, and out-of-scope items proactively
  • Strategy + Factory for splits, implemented and explained — including why the factory is separate from the strategy (creation vs. behavior)
  • Greedy two-heap settlement with explicit O(n log n) complexity analysis and the invariant that the sum of net balances is always zero
  • Observer pattern for notifications, with the explicit reasoning: "Group depends on the abstract observer interface, not on any concrete notification service"
  • Facade pattern for ExpenseManager, explained as: "callers interact with one surface; they don't know that SplitFactory, SettlementEngine, and Group exist"
  • Openness about tradeoffs: "The incremental pairwise dict is O(1) for lookups but requires careful dual-write discipline. An alternative is to recompute net balances on demand, trading write simplicity for read cost."
  • Extensibility answers should be specific: adding a new split type = one new class, one new factory method, one new elif. No existing logic changes. State this explicitly.
  • Verification: trace through a concrete example after implementing settlement. This is a habit, not a requirement — but it signals engineering maturity.

The senior signal isn't knowing more patterns. It's explaining why each pattern was chosen for this specific problem, and not using a pattern where it isn't needed.
