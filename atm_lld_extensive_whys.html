Step 1 · ~5 minutes
Requirements & Clarifications
The interviewer gives you a sentence. Your first job is to interrogate it — not design. An ATM sounds like a solved problem you use every day, and that familiarity is a trap. Every person who has used an ATM thinks they know what an ATM does, which means people make assumptions they never verbalize — and those assumptions become wrong classes. Below is how to dismantle the prompt with precision.
The prompt you'll receive
"Design an ATM system. Users should be able to insert a card, authenticate, and perform banking operations like withdrawals."

This sentence leaves almost everything ambiguous. "Banking operations" could mean withdrawal, deposit, transfer, balance inquiry, mini statement, PIN change — or just one of these. "Authenticate" might mean PIN only, or PIN + OTP, or biometrics. "Users" might be human customers at a physical ATM, or this might be a distributed system with hundreds of ATMs talking to a central bank server. These aren't small distinctions — each changes the class hierarchy fundamentally.

Clarifying Questions — with deep reasoning

You
"What operations should the ATM support — just withdrawal, or also deposit, transfer, balance inquiry, and PIN change?"
Why this is the most important first question: Each operation is a separate method on your ATMSystem or transaction class. More importantly, each operation changes what you need to model. Deposits require reasoning about how physical cash enters the machine. Transfers require a target account. PIN change requires verifying the old PIN and persisting the new one. If you assume "just withdrawal" and then the interviewer says "and deposits," you've built the wrong class. This question also reveals scope: if the interviewer says "just withdrawal and balance inquiry," you can design a tight, clean system. If they say "everything," you may need to propose which to design in depth and which to sketch. Never start drawing class boxes without knowing this.
Interviewer
"Support withdrawal, deposit, balance inquiry, and PIN change. Focus your deep design on withdrawal."
You
"Is this a single ATM with its own in-memory state, or are we modeling a distributed network of ATMs all talking to a central bank server?"
Why this changes your entire architecture: A standalone ATM holds account balances internally — your Account class has a balance field and is the source of truth. A networked ATM is just a terminal: it has no business logic about balances. It sends requests to a BankServer API and waits for responses. The second model requires a network layer, retry logic, timeout handling, and the ATM's behavior on server unavailability ("allow up to $200 offline?"). These two designs are not refinements of each other — they are different architectures. Knowing which one before you draw your first class saves you from designing the wrong system entirely.
Interviewer
"Model it as a standalone ATM — keep account state in memory. No need for a real bank server."
You
"How does authentication work — is it just a PIN, or are there multiple factors? And how many incorrect PIN attempts before the card is blocked?"
Why "how many attempts" is a design constraint, not a detail: The attempt limit drives a counter field on your Card or Account entity. Without it, you have no state to check. "3 failed attempts → card blocked" also forces a state machine decision: does blocking happen on the Card (CardStatus.BLOCKED) or on the Account (AccountStatus.LOCKED)? These are different business concepts — a card can be blocked while the account is fine (user's physical card was skimmed; they get a replacement card). If you don't ask this, you might model it either way, and the interviewer's follow-up question about card replacement will break your design.
Interviewer
"PIN only. Three wrong attempts blocks the card. Card block, not account lock."
You
"For withdrawals — does the ATM have a cash inventory it tracks? What happens if the machine is out of cash?"
Why the ATM's cash inventory is a first-class entity: Most junior designers model the ATM as a simple conduit — you ask for money, money comes out. But a real ATM has physical cash of specific denominations. Whether or not we track denominations determines whether CashDispenser is a serious class or just a side-note. It also reveals the need for a dispense(amount) method that must check if enough cash of the right denominations is available. If we track denominations, we need a "greedy" dispensing algorithm. If we don't, we just need a total-cash counter. Either way the ATM can run out of money — which is an error state that must be handled gracefully ("currently unavailable") and is completely different from "your account doesn't have that balance."
Interviewer
"Yes, track denominations — e.g., ₹500 and ₹100 notes. Dispense optimally. Handle insufficient cash gracefully."
You
"What happens to the session if the user takes too long between steps — like forgets to take their card after a transaction?"
Why session timeout matters for your state machine: A session has states: IDLE → CARD_INSERTED → AUTHENTICATED → TRANSACTION_IN_PROGRESS → COMPLETE. A timeout is a transition that can happen from any non-IDLE state back to IDLE. Without this, your ATM has no way to recover from a user who walks away mid-session. The card stays "logically inserted" forever. No other user can use the machine. In your LLD, even if you don't implement an actual timer, you need to show you understand that the state machine has a timeout transition — and that the ATM must eject the card and reset to IDLE when it fires. This shows systems thinking beyond the happy path.
Interviewer
"Good point — yes, include session timeout. The ATM resets to IDLE and ejects the card."
You
"Should we model transaction history — does the ATM keep a log of past transactions per account?"
Why this determines whether Transaction is a class or just a temporary value: If there's no history, a "transaction" is just the act of doing something — a method call with a result. You don't need a Transaction class at all. But if you need history, Transaction is a persistent entity with an ID, timestamp, amount, type, and status. It must be stored somewhere. The Account must hold a transaction_history: list[Transaction]. This distinction is the difference between 4 classes and 6 classes. Also, the mini-statement feature only makes sense if transactions are stored. Since they mentioned "balance inquiry," confirming history clarifies whether the fourth operation is secretly "mini-statement."
Interviewer
"Yes, maintain transaction history per account. Each transaction should have an ID, timestamp, type, amount, and status."

Scope Decision — what's in and what's out

  • Card insertion and authentication via PIN (3-attempt limit → card blocked)
  • Session management with states: IDLE → CARD_INSERTED → AUTHENTICATED → TRANSACTION_IN_PROGRESS → COMPLETE
  • Withdrawal with denomination-aware cash dispensing (greedy algorithm)
  • Deposit — adds cash to account balance
  • Balance inquiry — reads and displays current balance
  • PIN change — validates old PIN, sets new PIN
  • Transaction history per account (ID, timestamp, type, amount, status)
  • ATM cash inventory tracking with graceful "insufficient cash" handling
  • Multi-ATM network communication or bank server API
  • Real hardware interfaces (card reader, receipt printer drivers)
  • Multi-factor authentication (OTP, biometrics)
  • Inter-bank transfers or NEFT/UPI
  • Real-time fraud detection or AML compliance
Step 2 · ~8 minutes
Core Entities — Every Choice Justified
Entity design is the most consequential step. Pick the wrong entities and you spend the rest of the interview fighting your own classes. The rule of thumb: an entity is a class if it (1) has behavior (methods that enforce rules), or (2) has state that changes over time in meaningful ways. Anything else is a dataclass or a field on another class.
EntityTypeWhy it's its own class — and why not simpler
ClassATM Orchestrator The ATM is the single entry point — the facade for all user interactions. It holds the Session, the CashDispenser, and coordinates all operations. Without this class, who owns the state machine? Who decides whether a card insertion is valid? Who coordinates PIN validation with the Bank? An ATM is not just data — it actively manages state transitions and routes user actions to the right handler. It is the closest thing to a "controller" in this system.
ClassCard Entity A Card is not just a card number. It has state — CardStatus (ACTIVE, BLOCKED, EXPIRED). It has behavior — block(), validate_pin(entered_pin), and tracking failed_pin_attempts. It enforces rules: "you cannot exceed 3 wrong PINs," "an EXPIRED card cannot be used." These rules live on Card, not on the caller. If Card were just a string (the card number), who would own these rules? They'd scatter across ATM and Account — which means duplicated checks and bugs when one copy gets updated but another doesn't.
ClassAccount Entity The Account holds the financial truth of this system — balance and transaction history. It has behavior that enforces financial rules: debit(amount) must verify sufficient balance before modifying it. credit(amount) must reject negative amounts. These are guards — if they lived in ATM instead, every caller could bypass them. Account also owns transaction_history, which makes it the authoritative ledger. Keeping balance and history together on Account means you can never have a transaction history that contradicts the balance (both change in the same method call).
ClassSession Entity A session tracks who is using the ATM right now and at what stage. It is a state machine with states: IDLE → CARD_INSERTED → AUTHENTICATED → TRANSACTION_IN_PROGRESS → COMPLETE. Without a Session class, where does the current authenticated user live? You'd put current_card and current_account directly on ATM, but then ATM becomes responsible for both machine management AND session lifecycle — two concerns. Session separates "who is using this machine right now" from "what can this machine do." It also naturally holds the timeout timer and the current user's context.
ClassCashDispenser Entity CashDispenser encapsulates the physical cash the ATM holds. Its behavior is non-trivial: given an amount, determine which combination of denominations to dispense using a greedy algorithm, validate total cash availability, and update inventory after dispensing. If this logic lived on ATM, the ATM class would grow to include financial math, inventory management, and session management simultaneously. CashDispenser also makes the denomination algorithm swappable — an important extensibility point. It knows one thing deeply: how to turn a number into physical notes.
ClassTransaction Entity A Transaction is a persistent record of a financial event. It has an ID (for audit trails), a timestamp (for ordering history), a type (WITHDRAWAL, DEPOSIT, etc.), an amount, and a status (SUCCESS, FAILED). It is immutable after creation — once a withdrawal happened, you never modify the transaction record; you create a new one if a reversal occurs. Without Transaction as its own class, you'd store this as ad-hoc strings or tuples in a list — which loses type safety, makes queries impossible, and makes the code unreadable. Transaction is also an excellent candidate for a dataclass since it's mostly data with no mutable behavior after construction.
ClassBank Registry / Service Bank is the system's directory — it maps card numbers to accounts and manages account creation. Without Bank, who validates whether a card number corresponds to a real account? ATM would need a dict[card_number → Account], which is a repository pattern that deserves its own class. Bank also cleanly represents the separation between "the machine" (ATM) and "the financial institution" — even in our simplified model. In a real system, Bank would be replaced by an API client calling the core banking system. By giving it its own class now, you make that replacement a single-file change.
SkipAddress / CustomerInfo @dataclass Customer information (name, phone, email) is pure data — no behavior, no rules. A @dataclass is the right tool. You won't write methods on CustomerInfo. If you promoted it to a full class, you'd add empty method placeholders that mislead future readers into thinking it has behavior it doesn't. The one-question test: "Would this thing ever need to enforce a rule on its own data?" No. It is a dataclass.
Why Session and ATM are separate classes — the key mental model

Think about what each class answers when you ask it a question. ATM answers: "What hardware do I have? What can I physically do? Am I operational?" Session answers: "Who is currently using me? What stage are they at? What have they been authenticated for?" These are different questions with different lifecycles. An ATM outlives any individual session — it exists for years. A Session lives for one customer interaction — minutes. When the customer walks away, the Session dies. The ATM stays. Mixing them means you have to manually "clear" customer state from the ATM after each session — a side effect that's easy to forget and hard to test. Session makes that cleanup automatic: just create a new Session object.

Step 3 · ~15 minutes
Class Design — Every Line Justified
In every well-structured LLD there is one orchestrator. Here it is ATM. Everything is designed top-down: first the orchestrator, then the most complex class (Account), then the entities with the most interesting behavior (Card, CashDispenser, Session). For every field, ask: "what breaks if this field doesn't exist?" For every method: "who else would own this if I removed it?"

ATM — the Single Entry Point

ATM is the facade. External code (your main script, a UI) interacts only with ATM. Internally it delegates to Card, Account, CashDispenser, and Session. It does not implement business rules — it coordinates the entities that do. Without this class, callers must reason about "do I call Card.validate_pin() directly, then Account.debit()?" — coupling the caller to implementation details.

ATM — fields and public interface
class ATM: bank: Bank cash_dispenser: CashDispenser session: Session | None = None
Why inject Bank and CashDispenser instead of creating them? Dependency Injection. ATM receives its dependencies rather than creating them internally. This means: (1) In tests, you can inject a FakeBank that returns predetermined accounts. (2) You can inject a MockCashDispenser that records what it was asked to dispense. (3) The ATM is testable without any real bank or physical hardware. If ATM did self.bank = Bank() internally, you can never separate ATM logic from Bank logic in a test.
Without DI: Testing withdraw() requires a real Bank with real accounts. Every test is an integration test. A bug in Bank breaks all ATM tests even if ATM logic is correct.
session: Session | None
Why Session | None and not always-present Session? When no card is inserted, there is no active session. None makes this explicit — any code that tries to perform an operation without a session encounters a type-checker warning or a runtime AttributeError, not a silent wrong result. An always-present "empty" Session object would silently allow operations to proceed in an invalid state — the ATM would think it's authenticated when it isn't. None is the honest representation of "nobody is using this machine right now."
def insert_card(self, card_number: str) → bool
Why return bool, not raise an exception? Card insertion failure ("card not recognized") is a normal, expected event — someone inserts a card from a different bank, or a demagnetized card. Exceptions should signal unexpected failures, not routine user errors. A False return signals "try again or contact your bank," which the UI can display cleanly. Reserve exceptions for true errors — hardware failure, database unreachable, code bugs. This distinction (expected failures → return values; unexpected errors → exceptions) is a hallmark of well-designed interfaces.
If you raise on invalid card: UI code must wrap insert_card in try/except for normal flow — making normal operation look like error handling. The happy path and the expected-failure path look identical in the code structure.
def authenticate(self, pin: str) → bool
Why does ATM.authenticate() not directly check the PIN? ATM delegates to session.card.validate_pin(pin). The PIN validation logic — including the 3-attempt counter and card-blocking — lives on Card, not ATM. If ATM checked the PIN directly, the attempt counter would need to live on ATM, which means it's associated with the machine, not the card. But blocking is a property of the card — you can replace a blocked card; you can't replace the ATM. Delegation preserves the right ownership: ATM orchestrates, Card enforces.
def withdraw(self, amount: int) → TransactionResult def deposit(self, amount: int) → TransactionResult def check_balance(self) → Decimal def change_pin(self, old_pin: str, new_pin: str) → bool
Why amount: int for withdrawal, not float? Cash is dispensed in whole notes. You cannot withdraw ₹500.50. Accepting a float invites floating-point errors: 200.0 + 100.0 can produce 299.9999... in binary floating point, and comparing that to account balance produces wrong results. Use int (amounts in paise/cents) or Decimal for monetary values. int for withdrawal amounts is clean — it matches the physical reality that notes come in whole denominations. Balances use Decimal because they may include interest or fractional paise.
float for money: Classic production bug — user has balance 999.99, withdraws 1000.0, balance check computes to -0.0099999... which is < 0, so withdrawal incorrectly fails. Or worse: rounds in the wrong direction and allows an overdraft.
def eject_card(self) → None
Why a dedicated eject method instead of just setting session=None? Ejecting a card involves multiple cleanup steps: (1) mark session as terminated, (2) eject physical card (hardware call), (3) set self.session = None. If you just write self.session = None everywhere, the hardware call gets forgotten in some code paths. A dedicated eject_card() ensures all cleanup happens in one place. This is the same principle as Agent's complete_delivery() — atomic operations that must happen together belong in one method, never done piecemeal by callers.

Card — The State Machine for Physical Access

Card — status, PIN, attempt tracking
class CardStatus(Enum): ACTIVE = auto() BLOCKED = auto() EXPIRED = auto()
Why Enum, not a boolean is_blocked? A boolean captures one distinction: blocked or not. But there are three meaningfully different states — ACTIVE (usable), BLOCKED (too many wrong PINs — can be unblocked by calling the bank), EXPIRED (past validity date — must be replaced, cannot be unblocked). These states have different recovery paths. If you use a boolean, BLOCKED and EXPIRED both appear as True, and the code that handles them must ask a second question ("is it expired?") — re-creating state complexity you tried to hide. An Enum makes all three states explicit and unambiguous.
card_number: str # not int pin_hash: str failed_attempts: int = 0 MAX_ATTEMPTS: int = 3
Why store pin_hash, not the PIN itself? Never store PINs in plaintext — even in an interview LLD. The habit matters. In production, a database leak exposing plaintext PINs is catastrophic. We store a one-way hash (hashlib.sha256(pin.encode()).hexdigest()). When the user enters a PIN, we hash their input and compare hashes. The original PIN is never recoverable from storage. In an interview, mentioning this shows security awareness even in a design that isn't production-grade.
Plaintext PIN stored: Attacker gets DB read access. Every account is compromised. This has happened to real banks.
Hashed PIN: Attacker gets the hash. They cannot reverse it to get the PIN. Accounts remain safe.
def validate_pin(self, entered: str) -> bool: if self.status != ACTIVE: raise CardNotActiveError() hashed = hash_pin(entered) if hashed == self.pin_hash: self.failed_attempts = 0 return True self.failed_attempts += 1 if self.failed_attempts >= 3: self.block() return False
Why reset failed_attempts on success? Without resetting, a user who enters 2 wrong PINs, then the correct one, then later enters 1 wrong PIN — gets blocked. Their total lifetime errors sum to 3. That is wrong. The 3-attempt limit is per consecutive failed attempts — a protection against brute force. Once the correct PIN is entered, the brute force attempt is over. The counter resets. Not resetting this is a subtle bug that makes legitimate users get blocked after a single future mistake.
def block(self): self.status = CardStatus.BLOCKED self.failed_attempts = 0
Why a dedicated block() method instead of setting status directly? Today, blocking just changes status and resets the counter. Tomorrow, blocking might also: (1) send an SMS alert to the customer, (2) log the event to a fraud detection system, (3) notify the bank server. All of these are one-line additions to block(). If you had 3 places that each wrote self.status = BLOCKED, you would need to find and update all 3. One method, one update. This is the Tell, Don't Ask principle — tell the Card to block itself; don't ask for its fields and modify them externally.

Account — The Financial Ledger

Account — balance, guards, history
balance: Decimal transaction_history: list[Transaction]
Why Decimal for balance, not float? Bank balances include fractional rupees (interest, for example). float in Python uses binary floating-point, which cannot represent values like 0.1 exactly — 0.1 + 0.2 == 0.30000000000000004 in Python. Decimal is exact decimal arithmetic. For financial calculations, this difference between "almost right" and "exactly right" is the difference between a correct and an incorrect bank statement. One wrong cent per transaction compounds. Use Decimal for all monetary values.
def debit(self, amount: Decimal, txn_type: TransactionType ) -> Transaction: if amount <= 0: raise InvalidAmountError() if amount > self.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError() self.balance -= amount txn = Transaction( amount=-amount, txn_type=txn_type, status=SUCCESS) self.transaction_history.append(txn) return txn
Why validate amount > 0 inside debit(), when ATM already checked? Design by Contract: every method validates its own preconditions regardless of what the caller claims to have checked. A second entry point added 6 months later — an admin console, a scheduled fee deduction — may bypass ATM's validation. The guard inside debit() catches it regardless. This rule cost one line. The bug it prevents — a negative-amount debit that accidentally credits the account — would take days to diagnose in production. Never trust callers. Always validate at the point of mutation.
# Balance and history updated # atomically in the same method self.balance -= amount self.transaction_history.append(txn)
Why update balance and record history in the same method? These two operations must always happen together. A debit without a transaction record produces a balance that nobody can explain — the money is gone but there's no audit trail. A transaction record without a balance update creates a phantom transaction. By co-locating both mutations in debit(), they are either both done or neither is. This is the "atomic operation" principle at the class level — the Python equivalent of a database transaction.
If separated: ATM calls account.balance -= amount, then something throws an exception before transaction_history.append(). Balance is reduced, history is missing. Customer disputes the charge; bank has no record to defend itself.

CashDispenser — Denomination-Aware Dispensing

CashDispenser — inventory, greedy algorithm
denominations: dict[int, int] # e.g. {500: 20, 100: 50, 50: 10} # denomination → count of notes
Why dict[int, int] — denomination to count — not a list? A dict gives O(1) lookup by denomination. When checking "do I have ₹500 notes?", self.denominations[500] is instant. A list would require scanning for the right denomination every time. More importantly, a dict makes the denomination amounts explicit keys — you can't accidentally look up the wrong denomination. The dict structure also makes adding or removing denominations trivial: one dict update, no code changes elsewhere. This is the "flexible by design" approach — making change easy by choosing the right data structure.
def dispense(self, amount: int) -> dict[int,int]: # Phase 1: Validate if amount > self.total_cash(): raise InsufficientCashError() result = {} remaining = amount # Phase 2: Greedy algorithm for denom in sorted( self.denominations, reverse=True): count = min(remaining // denom, self.denominations[denom]) if count > 0: result[denom] = count remaining -= denom * count if remaining > 0: raise CannotDispenseExactError() # Phase 3: Deduct inventory for denom, count in result.items(): self.denominations[denom] -= count return result
Why validate total cash before the greedy loop — two separate errors? There are two distinct failure modes: (1) ATM doesn't have enough total cash — raise InsufficientCashError. Show "ATM currently unavailable." (2) ATM has enough cash but not the right combination of denominations to make the exact amount — e.g., amount=₹150 but only ₹500 notes remain. Raise CannotDispenseExactError. Show "Cannot dispense this exact amount." These errors have different recovery actions: the first sends a technician, the second might allow the user to request a different amount. One generic "dispensing error" loses the distinction that drives those different responses.
One generic error: User requests ₹750 from an ATM with ₹500 notes only. Code raises the same "error" as "ATM is empty." Bank's monitoring system treats it as "ATM out of cash" and sends a technician. The ATM had cash — it just needed the user to request ₹500 or ₹1000.
# Deduct AFTER successful computation for denom, count in result.items(): self.denominations[denom] -= count
Why deduct inventory only after the full computation succeeds? Two-phase design: compute everything first, mutate only if the computation succeeds entirely. If we deducted denomination by denomination inside the loop, and the loop discovered midway that exact change is impossible, we'd have partially deducted inventory with no corresponding dispensing. Getting inventory back to a consistent state would require rollback code — untested, complex, and fragile. Separating "figure out what to dispense" from "actually deduct it" eliminates the need for rollback entirely.

Session — The State Machine for User Interaction

Session — states, card/account refs, timeout
class SessionState(Enum): IDLE = auto() CARD_INSERTED = auto() AUTHENTICATED = auto() TXN_IN_PROGRESS = auto() COMPLETE = auto()
Why TXN_IN_PROGRESS as a separate state from AUTHENTICATED? AUTHENTICATED means "user proved identity." TXN_IN_PROGRESS means "user is currently executing a transaction." These are different states with different valid next actions. From AUTHENTICATED, valid operations are: start a transaction (→ TXN_IN_PROGRESS) or eject card (→ IDLE). From TXN_IN_PROGRESS, valid operations are: complete the transaction (→ AUTHENTICATED, allowing multiple transactions per session) or cancel. Collapsing them means you can't represent "user wants another transaction after the first one completes" without inventing ad-hoc booleans. The state machine makes multi-transaction sessions structurally natural.
card: Card | None account: Account | None state: SessionState session_id: str # UUID started_at: datetime
Why does Session hold both Card and Account references? After authentication, both are needed for every subsequent operation. Withdrawal needs the Account to check balance and debit. PIN change needs the Card to validate old PIN and store new one. Keeping both in Session avoids repeated Bank lookups mid-session — the lookups happened at card insertion and authentication; everything after uses the already-resolved references. Session is the "context" for the entire user interaction. This is the classic "session as context" pattern — load once, use many times.
VALID_TRANSITIONS = { IDLE: [CARD_INSERTED], CARD_INSERTED: [AUTHENTICATED, IDLE], AUTHENTICATED: [TXN_IN_PROGRESS, IDLE], TXN_IN_PROGRESS: [AUTHENTICATED, IDLE], COMPLETE: [] }
Why does TXN_IN_PROGRESS go back to AUTHENTICATED (not COMPLETE)? A real ATM session allows multiple transactions: "Withdraw ₹2000, then check balance, then change PIN — all without re-inserting the card." After each transaction completes, the session returns to AUTHENTICATED, allowing the user to choose another operation. COMPLETE is reached only when the user explicitly selects "End Session" or the card is ejected. IDLE is reachable from any non-COMPLETE state via timeout or cancellation. This models actual ATM behavior and prevents the anti-pattern of forcing card re-insertion between each operation.

Transaction — Immutable Financial Record

Transaction — as a frozen dataclass
@dataclass(frozen=True) class Transaction: txn_id: str # UUID account_id: str txn_type: TransactionType amount: Decimal status: TransactionStatus timestamp: datetime = field( default_factory=datetime.now)
Why frozen=True on Transaction? frozen=True makes all fields immutable after creation — Python will raise a FrozenInstanceError if any code tries to modify a field. This enforces an important business rule: financial records are append-only. You never modify a transaction that happened. If a correction is needed, you create a reversal transaction. Freezing the dataclass makes this rule enforceable at the language level — a developer cannot accidentally write txn.amount = new_amount. The type system becomes your audit enforcer.
Without frozen: A bug in error handling code modifies a transaction's amount to 0 to "mark it cancelled" instead of creating a reversal. The original debit record is gone. Audit trail is corrupted. Regulators are not amused.
Step 4 · ~10 minutes
Key Flows — Step by Step
Understanding the happy path is table stakes. What separates good from great answers is knowing exactly what happens at each step, which entity owns each action, and what error handling is required. Below are the four core flows with step-by-step reasoning.

Flow 1 — Card Insertion and PIN Authentication

ATM.insert_card(card_number) then ATM.authenticate(pin)
Step 1: Check ATM state if self.session is not None: return False # already in use
Why check if session exists before allowing insertion? A physical ATM can only serve one user at a time. If a session already exists (someone's card is inside), a second card insertion must be rejected. Without this check, inserting a second card could corrupt the active session — overwriting the current user's card and account references mid-transaction. This is a guard for physical concurrency — a constraint that flows directly from the hardware reality of one card slot.
Step 2: Look up card card = self.bank.get_card(card_number) if card is None: return False # unknown card if card.status != CardStatus.ACTIVE: return False # blocked/expired
Why check card status before creating a session? Fail fast. If the card is BLOCKED or EXPIRED, there is no valid path forward — not even to CARD_INSERTED state. Creating a session for a blocked card wastes state and creates a path that will fail at authentication regardless. Checking status here prevents the user from entering their PIN (a UX dead-end) before being told their card is blocked. The earlier you surface an error, the cleaner the system and the faster the feedback to the user.
Step 3: Create session account = self.bank.get_account( card.account_id) self.session = Session( card=card, account=account, state=CARD_INSERTED)
Why resolve the Account at insertion, not at authentication? Authentication proves identity, but the account is associated with the card from the moment the card is identified. Once we know it's a valid card, we know which account it belongs to. Loading the account eagerly (at insertion) means all subsequent operations — even PIN validation feedback ("3 more attempts remaining") — have the account context they need. It also reduces Bank lookups: one call at insertion vs. a separate call per operation. In a real system, this would also "lock" the session record in the database, preventing concurrent sessions.
Step 4: Validate PIN (in authenticate()) result = self.session.card .validate_pin(entered_pin) if not result: if self.session.card.status == BLOCKED: self.eject_card() return False
Why check if card got blocked after PIN failure and eject immediately? When the third wrong PIN is entered, validate_pin() blocks the card and returns False. If we don't check whether the card got blocked, the session stays in CARD_INSERTED state — waiting for more PIN attempts that can never succeed (card is blocked, all future validate_pin() calls raise CardNotActiveError). Checking the post-call status and ejecting immediately returns the machine to IDLE — physically returning the card to the user with a "card blocked" message. Clean state, clear feedback.
Step 5: Session promotion self.session.transition_to(AUTHENTICATED) return True
Why use transition_to() instead of directly setting session.state? transition_to() validates the transition against VALID_TRANSITIONS before making it. Direct state assignment bypasses this check — any code path could put the session into any state. A bug that does session.state = AUTHENTICATED without going through PIN validation would create an authenticated session with no PIN check. transition_to() is the single enforcement point for the state machine's rules. This is exactly the same principle as Order's update_status() — enforce rules at the point of mutation, not in the caller.

Flow 2 — Withdrawal (happy path overview)

The full deep-dive is in Section 04b. Here's the high-level sequence showing which entity is responsible for each step:

ATM.withdraw(amount) — entity responsibilities
Step 1: Guard — session valid? self._require_authenticated()
Whose responsibility?ATM. All operations must first verify the session is in AUTHENTICATED state. This single internal guard method is called at the top of every protected operation. Centralizing it means you cannot accidentally write a withdrawal method that forgets to check authentication.
Step 2: Amount validation if amount <= 0 or amount % 100 != 0: raise InvalidAmountError()
Why check multiples of 100?ATM. Cash dispensers have minimum denomination constraints. If the smallest note is ₹100, the ATM literally cannot dispense ₹150. Checking this constraint before hitting the account or dispenser produces the right error message early: "Please enter a multiple of ₹100." Without this, the CashDispenser would raise CannotDispenseExactError — a more confusing message about denominations.
Step 3: Check account balance account = self.session.account if amount > account.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError()
Whose responsibility?ATM checks here for early exit, but Account.debit() also checks internally (design by contract). The check here gives a clean user-facing error before attempting to interact with the dispenser. A user with ₹500 balance trying to withdraw ₹2000 should be told immediately — don't even check the dispenser.
Step 4: Reserve cash notes = self.cash_dispenser.dispense(amount)
Why dispense before deducting account balance?CashDispenser. The physical cash must be available before the account is debited. If we debited first and then found the dispenser empty, we'd need to reverse the debit — a rollback. Dispensing first means if the dispenser fails, no account mutation has occurred. No rollback needed. The two-phase principle: validate/reserve physical resources first, then commit financial changes.
Step 5: Debit account txn = account.debit( Decimal(amount), WITHDRAWAL)
Whose responsibility?Account owns its own mutation. ATM doesn't directly modify account.balance — it calls account.debit() which validates, deducts, and records the transaction atomically. ATM gets back a Transaction object and nothing more.
Step 6: Transition session state self.session.transition_to( TXN_IN_PROGRESS) # during op # ... after completion: self.session.transition_to(AUTHENTICATED)
Why track TXN_IN_PROGRESS?Session. Marking the session as TXN_IN_PROGRESS prevents the user (or a concurrent thread in a real system) from initiating a second transaction before the first completes. Returning to AUTHENTICATED after completion allows further transactions in the same session — realistic behavior that also naturally enables the "would you like another transaction?" UX prompt.

Flow 3 — The Session State Machine (every transition justified)

IDLE→ CARD_INSERTED→ AUTHENTICATED→ TXN_IN_PROGRESS→ AUTHENTICATED→ COMPLETE

↳ IDLE is reachable from any state via timeout or explicit card ejection — the "reset to safe state" transition.

Why each transition is exactly what it is
IDLE → CARD_INSERTED only
Why can't IDLE go directly to AUTHENTICATED?Physical constraint: you cannot be authenticated without a card. Authentication requires a PIN entry, which requires a card to be present. The CARD_INSERTED state is not administrative overhead — it represents real physical reality: the card is in the slot. Skipping this state would allow authentication without a card, which is nonsensical.
CARD_INSERTED → AUTHENTICATED CARD_INSERTED → IDLE (ejection/timeout)
Why does CARD_INSERTED go back to IDLE?IDLE is the safe resting state. If the user inserts a card and then walks away, the timeout fires, the session goes CARD_INSERTED → IDLE, and the card is physically ejected. Without this transition, a forgotten card keeps the ATM "occupied" forever. The IDLE → anything → IDLE path being possible from every state is what makes the ATM resilient to abandoned sessions.
TXN_IN_PROGRESS → AUTHENTICATED (allows multiple transactions)
Why not TXN_IN_PROGRESS → COMPLETE directly?Returning to AUTHENTICATED after each transaction enables multi-operation sessions. The user might withdraw, then check balance, then change their PIN. Each operation is a round trip: AUTHENTICATED → TXN_IN_PROGRESS → AUTHENTICATED. COMPLETE is only reached when the user explicitly ends the session (selects "Done" on the screen) or the session times out from AUTHENTICATED. Real ATMs work exactly this way — the machine prompts "Would you like another transaction?" between operations.
Any state → IDLE via timeout
Why allow IDLE from any state (not just AUTHENTICATED)?Users can abandon the ATM at any stage — while entering their PIN, mid-transaction selection, even mid-PIN-change. The ATM must always be able to recover to a clean state. The timeout is the safety net. Making IDLE reachable from every state means the ATM is always self-healing: any abandoned session, any forgotten card, any stuck state resolves itself within the timeout window without human intervention.
Step 4b · Deep Dive
withdraw() — Bad to Great
Withdrawal is the most critical and complex operation in the ATM. It touches four entities, has multiple validation layers, and contains a sequencing decision — "deduct cash before or after debiting account?" — that, if wrong, introduces a class of bugs requiring production rollback code. Let's build it from scratch.
Bad Debit account first, then dispense cash

The intuitive approach

The "obvious" order: check balance, deduct from account, dispense cash. This feels right — deduct money, give money. But it creates a gap that is catastrophic in production.

def withdraw(self, amount): account = self.session.account # Step 1: Validate balance if amount > account.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError() # Step 2: Debit account FIRST txn = account.debit(amount, WITHDRAWAL) # ← money is gone # Step 3: Now try to dispense try: notes = self.cash_dispenser.dispense(amount) except InsufficientCashError: # 🔥 Account debited but no cash dispensed account.credit(amount) # ← rollback attempt raise ATMError("ATM out of cash")

Why this fails — two compounding problems

Bug 1 — The rollback is fragile: The account.credit(amount) "rollback" works in the happy case. But what if credit() itself throws an exception? (Disk full, server unavailable, any runtime error.) Now you have a debited account with no credit reversal and no cash dispensed. The customer has paid without receiving anything. This is the fundamental problem with "compensating transactions" — they're code that runs when things are already wrong, and if they also fail, you've made things worse.

Bug 2 — The transaction record is corrupted: The debit() call already appended a WITHDRAWAL transaction to account history. The attempted rollback via credit() adds a second CREDIT transaction. The net effect on balance is correct, but the customer's statement now shows a withdrawal and a mysterious credit — confusing and alarming. A clean "ATM out of cash" should produce no transaction record at all.

Production consequence: In a real system under load, a network partition between the debit (which succeeded) and the dispenser check (which fails due to timeout) produces an account that has been debited but no cash given. The customer calls the bank. The bank's logs show a successful debit. The ATM's logs show a dispensing failure. Reconciling these takes days — and this happens thousands of times per day across the banking system. This is why ATMs have specific transaction sequencing standards (ISO 8583).
Good Reserve cash first, then debit account

The correct insight

Physical resources (cash) must be reserved before financial records are committed. If the physical operation fails, no financial change should ever occur. Reverse the order: dispense first, debit after. The invariant you want is: "If the account is debited, cash was definitely dispensed."

def withdraw(self, amount: int) -> Transaction: # --- PHASE 1: Full validation (no mutations) --- self._require_authenticated() if amount <= 0 or amount % 100 != 0: raise InvalidAmountError( "Amount must be a positive multiple of ₹100") account = self.session.account if Decimal(amount) > account.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError( f"Balance: {account.balance}, requested: {amount}") # --- PHASE 2: Reserve physical resource first --- self.session.transition_to(TXN_IN_PROGRESS) notes = self.cash_dispenser.dispense(amount) # ↑ If this raises, NO financial record is created. # ↑ No rollback needed. Session stays clean. # --- PHASE 3: Commit financial change --- txn = account.debit(Decimal(amount), TransactionType.WITHDRAWAL) # ↑ Only reached if cash was successfully reserved. # ↑ Invariant: "if debit happened, cash was dispensed." self.session.transition_to(AUTHENTICATED) return txn

Why this is correct — the two guarantees it provides

Guarantee 1 — No rollback code: If cash_dispenser.dispense() raises for any reason (out of cash, wrong denominations, hardware fault), the exception propagates out before account.debit() is ever called. No debit happened. No transaction was recorded. No rollback is needed. The system is in a perfectly clean state: the session is back to AUTHENTICATED (the transition to TXN_IN_PROGRESS should be rolled back on exception too — handled in the exception handler), and the user can try again or contact support.

Guarantee 2 — The invariant is structural: "If account.debit() ran, then cash_dispenser.dispense() must have succeeded." This isn't a comment or a hope — it's a structural consequence of the code order. The debit line is unreachable unless dispense succeeded. Invariants enforced by code structure are infinitely stronger than invariants enforced by programmer discipline.

What to say in the interview: "I split withdraw() into three phases: pure validation, physical resource reservation, and financial commitment. The key sequencing decision is that cash dispensing comes before account debiting. This ensures that if the dispenser fails for any reason — out of cash, wrong denominations, hardware fault — no financial record is created and no rollback is needed. The invariant 'account was debited ↔ cash was dispensed' is structurally enforced by code order, not by programmer discipline."
Step 5 · Deep Dive
Design Patterns — Justified, Not Just Named
Name a pattern only when you can say: "Without this pattern, here is the specific problem I would face." Patterns are solutions to recurring problems, not decorations. Below, each pattern is introduced with its concrete problem, applied to the ATM domain, with before-vs-after code.
1. State Machine Pattern applied in Session

The specific problem without it: Every ATM operation needs to verify the session is in the right state. withdraw() needs AUTHENTICATED. authenticate() needs CARD_INSERTED. Without a state machine, each method has its own if/else check. When a new state (like TXN_IN_PROGRESS) is added, every method needs reviewing. The rules are scattered and easy to get wrong.

Without State Machine
def withdraw(self, amount):
    if not self.session:
        raise NoSessionError()
    if self.session.state != AUTHENTICATED
       and self.session.state != TXN_IN_PROGRESS:
        raise WrongStateError()
    # ... now do work

def change_pin(self, old, new):
    if not self.session:
        raise NoSessionError()
    if self.session.state != AUTHENTICATED:
        raise WrongStateError()
    # Different check!
Two methods, two different checks. When you add a new state, you must audit every method to see if it needs updating. In a 10-operation ATM, that's 10 checks to get right.
With State Machine
VALID_TRANSITIONS = {
    IDLE:           [CARD_INSERTED],
    CARD_INSERTED:  [AUTHENTICATED, IDLE],
    AUTHENTICATED:  [TXN_IN_PROGRESS, IDLE],
    TXN_IN_PROGRESS:[AUTHENTICATED, IDLE],
}

def transition_to(self, new_state):
    allowed = VALID_TRANSITIONS.get(
        self.state, [])
    if new_state not in allowed:
        raise InvalidTransitionError()
    self.state = new_state
All rules live in one dict. Adding TXN_IN_PROGRESS is one dict entry. Every method calls transition_to() and gets the right check automatically. Rules are data, not code.
2. Strategy Pattern applied in CashDispenser

The specific problem without it: The greedy denomination algorithm works for most cases, but different ATM operators may want different dispensing strategies — some prefer using larger notes first (greedy), others want to minimize total notes given (minimum-count), others may have regulatory constraints. If the algorithm is hardcoded in CashDispenser.dispense(), changing it requires modifying a class that already works correctly — risking regressions.

class DispenseStrategy(ABC): @abstractmethod def compute(self, amount: int, denominations: dict[int, int] ) -> dict[int, int]: ... class GreedyStrategy(DispenseStrategy): def compute(self, amount, denominations): # Sort denominations largest-first # Greedily take as many of each as possible ... class MinNotesStrategy(DispenseStrategy): def compute(self, amount, denominations): # Dynamic programming to minimize note count ... class CashDispenser: def __init__(self, strategy: DispenseStrategy = GreedyStrategy()): self.strategy = strategy def dispense(self, amount): notes = self.strategy.compute( amount, self.denominations) # validate then deduct...

The concrete benefit: Swapping dispensing algorithms requires no changes to CashDispenser — inject a different strategy. Tests can inject a MockStrategy that returns predetermined results regardless of the denominations. The algorithm and the inventory management are separated. In an interview, mentioning this shows you understand that algorithms are a separate concern from the class that hosts them.

3. Singleton Pattern applied in Bank

The specific problem without it: If Bank can be instantiated multiple times, you can have multiple in-memory stores of accounts and cards. Two ATM instances using two Bank instances would see different accounts — one update on one Bank would not be visible to the other. In our single-ATM model this isn't an issue, but the interviewer often asks "how would this extend to multiple ATMs?" Singleton on Bank ensures one shared registry regardless of how many ATM instances exist.

class Bank: _instance: 'Bank' | None = None def __new__(cls): if cls._instance is None: cls._instance = super().__new__(cls) cls._instance.accounts = {} cls._instance.cards = {} return cls._instance

Important caveat to mention in the interview: Singleton is appropriate here because Bank is stateful shared infrastructure. It is not appropriate for things like configuration or logging in a library — those should use dependency injection. The rule: use Singleton only when the "exactly one instance" property is a genuine business requirement (one shared bank, one printer queue) rather than a convenience shortcut. Be ready to explain why you chose Singleton over dependency injection here: because Bank's identity (same instance everywhere) is the point.

4. Template Method Pattern applied in ATM operations

The specific problem without it: Every ATM operation follows the same skeleton: (1) validate session is AUTHENTICATED, (2) transition to TXN_IN_PROGRESS, (3) do the operation-specific work, (4) transition back to AUTHENTICATED, (5) return result. Steps 1, 2, 4 are identical across all four operations. Without Template Method, you copy-paste these three steps into every operation — and when step 1's logic changes, you update it in 4 places.

class ATM: def _execute_transaction(self, operation): """Template: all transactions follow this skeleton.""" self._require_authenticated() self.session.transition_to(TXN_IN_PROGRESS) try: result = operation() # ← variable step self.session.transition_to(AUTHENTICATED) return result except Exception: self.session.transition_to(AUTHENTICATED) raise def withdraw(self, amount): return self._execute_transaction( lambda: self._do_withdraw(amount)) def check_balance(self): return self._execute_transaction( lambda: self.session.account.balance)

The concrete benefit: The session lifecycle (authenticate → in-progress → authenticate) is written once. Adding a new operation (say, "mini-statement") requires only adding the _do_mini_statement() method and wrapping it in _execute_transaction(). The template ensures it automatically gets the right state transitions. This is the Open/Closed Principle made concrete: the system is open for new operations, closed for changes to the shared skeleton.

5. Observer Pattern applied for transaction notifications

The specific problem without it: After a successful transaction, multiple things need to happen: the receipt prints, an SMS is sent to the customer, the bank's audit log is updated, potentially a fraud alert is triggered. If Account.debit() calls all of these directly, it has five dependencies — printing, SMS, audit, fraud — none of which are related to managing a bank account. Every new notification requirement (a new app push notification service) requires modifying Account, a core financial class.

class TransactionObserver(ABC): @abstractmethod def on_transaction(self, account: 'Account', txn: Transaction) -> None: ... class SMSNotifier(TransactionObserver): def on_transaction(self, account, txn): # send SMS: "₹{txn.amount} debited from account" ... class AuditLogger(TransactionObserver): def on_transaction(self, account, txn): # write to audit log with timestamp ... class Account: observers: list[TransactionObserver] def debit(self, amount, txn_type): # ... do the debit ... txn = Transaction(...) for obs in self.observers: obs.on_transaction(self, txn) return txn

The concrete benefit: Account knows nothing about SMS, printing, or fraud. It just calls on_transaction() on whoever is listening. Adding a new notification channel (push notifications) is a new class that implements TransactionObserver — Account never changes. In tests, inject a MockObserver that records calls, verify the right notifications fired without sending any real SMS.

Step 6 · Deep Dive
SOLID Principles — Applied to ATM
SOLID principles are only valuable if you can point to a specific consequence in your design. For each principle, here is: the definition, the concrete ATM scenario that violates it, and the specific change in your design that fixes it.
S
Single Responsibility Principle
"A class should have one reason to change."
Violation: Merging CashDispenser into ATM. Now ATM changes when: the denomination algorithm changes, the dispensing hardware API changes, the session logic changes, or a new operation is added. Four reasons to change.
Fix: CashDispenser is its own class. ATM changes only when the user-facing interaction model changes. CashDispenser changes only when denomination logic or hardware interface changes. The classes evolve independently.
O
Open/Closed Principle
"Open for extension, closed for modification."
Violation: Hardcoding the greedy dispensing algorithm in CashDispenser. To add min-notes or regulatory strategies, you modify existing tested code — risking regression.
Fix: DispenseStrategy abstract class + GreedyStrategy + MinNotesStrategy. New strategy = new class. CashDispenser never changes. Existing tests pass unchanged.
L
Liskov Substitution Principle
"Subtypes must be substitutable for their base type."
Violation: A PremiumDispenseStrategy subclass that overrides compute() but raises an exception when the amount is above ₹10,000. The base class contract promises a result for any valid amount. The subclass breaks it.
Fix: All DispenseStrategy implementations must handle the full contract: return valid notes for any dispensable amount, raise InsufficientCashError or CannotDispenseExactError only for genuinely impossible requests — no new exception types that callers don't know to catch.
I
Interface Segregation Principle
"Clients should not be forced to depend on methods they don't use."
Violation: A single BankService interface with 15 methods — card lookup, account lookup, inter-bank transfer, fraud scoring, KYC verification. ATM only needs 3 of these but must "know about" all 15.
Fix: Split into CardRepository (get_card, block_card), AccountRepository (get_account, update_balance). ATM depends only on these two focused interfaces — not on the full bank service surface area.
D
Dependency Inversion Principle
"High-level modules should not depend on low-level modules. Both should depend on abstractions."
Violation: ATM creates its own Bank and CashDispenser internally: self.bank = Bank(), self.cash_dispenser = CashDispenser(). ATM is now coupled to the concrete implementations. You cannot test ATM without running real Bank and CashDispenser code. You cannot replace Bank with a different implementation.
Fix: ATM receives its dependencies through the constructor: ATM(bank: Bank, cash_dispenser: CashDispenser). In tests, inject FakeBank (returns predetermined accounts) and MockCashDispenser (records calls). In production, inject the real implementations. ATM logic is testable in pure isolation. This is why the four patterns (Strategy, Observer, Template, Singleton) work cleanly — they depend on abstractions, not concretions.
Step 7 · Discussions
Trade-offs — Every Design Decision Has a Cost
A senior engineer doesn't just make decisions — they articulate the tradeoffs of those decisions. For every design choice below, here is why you made it, what you gave up, and the scenario where the opposite choice would be correct.
Session as a separate class vs. fields on ATM
The tradeoff: We gave Session its own class (with its own state, card ref, account ref, timeout). The alternative is storing current_card, current_account, session_state directly as fields on ATM.
Chose: Separate Session class Clean lifecycle: creating a new Session object at card insertion and discarding it at ejection is self-documenting. Session's state machine is isolated and testable independently. Adding a session-level field (like session duration timer) doesn't touch ATM.
Cost One more class to explain. Object creation/deletion on each session is slightly more overhead. For a toy system with no complexity beyond the happy path, fields on ATM are simpler.

When you'd choose otherwise: If the scope was "design a simple ATM for one operation only — withdrawal," the separate Session class might be over-engineering. With four operations, timeout, and multi-transaction sessions, Session earns its existence.

Denomination tracking vs. simple total-cash counter
The tradeoff: We model CashDispenser with dict[denomination → count] and a greedy algorithm. The alternative is a single total_cash: int field — deduct requested amount, raise error if insufficient.
Chose: Denomination tracking Models physical reality. Catches the case where ATM has ₹10,000 cash but only in ₹2,000 notes and you request ₹500. Enables ATM monitoring ("ATM low on ₹100 notes, restock soon"). Interview-grade design shows awareness of real-world constraints.
Cost Greedy algorithm is O(k) where k is number of denomination types (small constant, irrelevant). But CannotDispenseExactError introduces a failure mode that doesn't exist in the simple model — the ATM has cash but can't make change. More complex to explain.

When you'd choose otherwise: If the interviewer says "keep it simple, just check if ATM has enough cash," use the simple counter. Don't add complexity that wasn't asked for. The right answer depends on the scope question you asked in Section 1.

Block on Card vs. block on Account
The tradeoff: We block the Card after 3 wrong PINs (CardStatus.BLOCKED), not the Account. The alternative is locking the Account.
Chose: Block on Card Correctly models reality: card skimming → block this card, issue new card, account is fine. A blocked card can be replaced; the account never goes offline. Customer can immediately use internet banking or walk into a branch while waiting for the new card.
Cost Slightly more complex: Bank needs to issue a new Card and link it to the existing Account. If you block on Account instead, card replacement is simpler (just reset the account) but the account goes offline — worse for the customer.

When you'd choose otherwise: If the threat model is "someone knows the customer's credentials and is trying to log in via online banking," you'd lock the Account. The distinction: physical card theft → block Card. Credential compromise → lock Account. Your design should support both independently.

In-memory state vs. database-backed persistence
The tradeoff: We hold accounts, cards, and transactions in dictionaries in memory (our Bank class is an in-memory store). In production, these would be backed by a database.
Chose: In-memory for LLD scope Clean class design. No persistence layer to reason about. Focused on showing entity relationships and behavior. All interview evaluations of LLD focus on class design, not database schema.
Cost All data is lost on restart. No concurrency safety — two simultaneous withdrawals could both read the same balance before either writes. In production, both are solved by a database with transaction isolation.

How to extend it: The clean class design means swapping in-memory storage for database calls is additive, not rewriting. Replace self.accounts[id] with db.query("SELECT * FROM accounts WHERE id = %s", id). The class interfaces don't change — only the storage layer changes. This is the Repository pattern — and our Bank class is already shaped like a repository.

Step 8 · Interview Prep
Hard Interview Questions — Fully Answered
These are the follow-up questions that separate candidates who know the surface design from those who understand the reasoning. For each, there is a concise answer and the key insight the interviewer is probing for.
What happens if the ATM dispenses cash but the network goes down before the account debit is recorded?

This is the hardest failure scenario in ATM design — it's called the "dispense-but-no-debit" problem. In our LLD, it can't happen because we're in-memory and there's no network. But in a production system this is why ATMs maintain a local transaction journal.

The ATM writes a "pending dispense" record to its local disk before dispensing cash. After dispensing, it marks the record "complete." When the network comes back, the ATM sends all pending/incomplete transactions to the bank for reconciliation. If the ATM crashed mid-dispense, the journal shows an incomplete record — the bank investigates whether cash was actually given.

The key insight: in distributed systems, you cannot have atomicity across a network partition without a protocol. ATMs use a "log first, act second" (write-ahead logging) approach. In an LLD interview, showing you know this failure mode exists — even if you can't implement it — demonstrates real production thinking.

Why is failed_attempts on Card, not on Session?

This is a Tell, Don't Ask question in disguise. The 3-attempt limit is a property of the card, not a property of a particular session.

If attempts were tracked on Session: a malicious user inserts their blocked card, gets rejected at 3 attempts, physically removes and re-inserts the card (starting a new Session), and gets 3 more attempts. Repeat. The session-level counter resets with each insertion. The attacker gets unlimited PIN attempts — the entire security mechanism is defeated.

With attempts on Card: re-inserting the card doesn't reset the counter. card.failed_attempts persists across sessions because it's a field on the Card object, not the Session object. The card gets blocked regardless of how many sessions it creates. The security guarantee is unconditional.

The general rule: put state on the entity whose properties it describes. Card blocking is about the card. Session is just the current interaction context.

How would you add support for a transfer operation between two accounts?

This tests whether your design is extensible without major surgery. Here's the minimal change set:

New operation on ATM: transfer(amount: int, target_account_id: str) → Transaction. Same session guard and TXN_IN_PROGRESS transition as other operations via _execute_transaction().

The critical sequencing problem: You must debit source and credit destination atomically. If debit succeeds but credit fails (target account not found), you have lost money. The solution: validate both accounts exist before touching either balance. This is the two-phase pattern again — validate everything, then mutate.

def _do_transfer(self, amount, target_id): source = self.session.account target = self.bank.get_account(target_id) if target is None: raise AccountNotFoundError() # validate first if Decimal(amount) > source.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError() # Both valid — now mutate txn_out = source.debit(amount, TRANSFER_OUT) txn_in = target.credit(amount, TRANSFER_IN) return txn_out

On CashDispenser: No changes. Transfers don't dispense cash. This is a key sign that CashDispenser was correctly separated — a new financial operation doesn't touch it.

On TransactionType enum: Add TRANSFER_IN and TRANSFER_OUT. This is one line. The existing enum handling in debit() and credit() works unchanged — they accept any TransactionType.

No other class changes. This is what "designed for extension" looks like in practice.

Why does ATM.withdraw() check balance before calling cash_dispenser.dispense()? Why not let dispense handle everything?

This tests your understanding of where business rules belong. The balance check is an account rule — "you cannot withdraw more than you have." CashDispenser knows nothing about accounts; it only knows about physical cash. If you moved the balance check into CashDispenser, you'd be giving CashDispenser a dependency on Account — a machine part that knows about your bank balance. That's a wrong coupling.

More practically: if the user has ₹200 balance and requests ₹5000, the right error is "Insufficient funds" — an account-level error. If we skipped the balance check and let CashDispenser try to dispense ₹5000, it would succeed (the machine might have the cash), then Account.debit() would fail — a confusing error that came from the wrong place. Error messages should come from the entity that owns the rule.

The sequencing in withdraw() puts each check in the right entity in the right order: ATM validates session and amount format, Account validates balance (via the ATM's pre-check and Account's own guard), CashDispenser validates physical availability. Three entities, three domains of responsibility, none overstepping.

How would you make this design thread-safe for a multi-threaded environment?

There are two sources of concurrency risk in this design:

1. Account balance — the classic race condition: Two threads call account.debit(1000) simultaneously. Both read balance = 1500. Both check 1000 < 1500 → True. Both deduct. Balance becomes 500. The account gave out ₹2000 but only had ₹1500. Fix: add a threading lock to Account:

import threading class Account: def __init__(self, ...): self._lock = threading.Lock() def debit(self, amount, txn_type): with self._lock: # atomic read-check-write if amount > self.balance: raise InsufficientFundsError() self.balance -= amount ...

2. CashDispenser inventory — the same race: Two withdrawals both compute a valid dispensing plan from the same inventory state, then both try to deduct. Same fix — a lock on CashDispenser's dispense().

The key insight for the interviewer: Our two-phase design (compute first, deduct after) reduces but doesn't eliminate the race condition window. In a distributed system (multiple ATMs hitting one database), you'd need database-level locking — pessimistic (SELECT FOR UPDATE) or optimistic (compare-and-swap on the balance column). In-memory with threading, the Lock suffices.

Walk me through the complete sequence when a withdrawal fails because the ATM is out of cash.

Here's every step, the responsible entity, and what state the system is in at each point:

1. Session guard: ATM._require_authenticated() passes — user is authenticated. Session transitions AUTHENTICATED → TXN_IN_PROGRESS.

2. Amount validation: ATM checks amount is positive and a multiple of ₹100. Passes.

3. Balance check: ATM checks amount <= account.balance. Passes — user has sufficient funds.

4. Dispensing attempt: cash_dispenser.dispense(amount) is called. CashDispenser computes total_cash() — it's less than the requested amount. Raises InsufficientCashError.

5. Exception propagation: The exception exits _execute_transaction()'s try block. The except clause transitions the session back: TXN_IN_PROGRESS → AUTHENTICATED. No account.debit() was called. No Transaction was created. The account balance is unchanged.

6. ATM displays message: "This ATM is temporarily unable to process your request. Please try another ATM." The user can then select another operation or eject their card.

The key takeaway: Because we dispensed before debiting (the two-phase ordering decision), the system is in a perfectly clean state after the failure. No partial mutations. No orphaned transaction records. No need for compensating transactions. The account has its full balance, the session is back to AUTHENTICATED, and the user can try a different amount or walk to a different ATM. This is what correct failure handling looks like.
